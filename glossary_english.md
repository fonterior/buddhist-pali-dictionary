
# BUDDHIST DICTIONARY OF THE PĀLI TRADITION

*Based on suttas of the Pāli Canon (Dīgha-nikāya — DN, Majjhima-nikāya — MN, Saṃyutta-nikāya — SN, Aṅguttara-nikāya — AN)*

---

## From the Author

This is a Buddhist dictionary of the Pāli tradition — a reference work of technical terms and a tool for delving into the Dhamma, the Buddha's teaching preserved in the Pāli Canon.

**Who will find this dictionary useful?**

**Practitioners:** To understand which states of mind arise in practice and how to work with them.

**Students of the sources:** To grasp the true meaning of a term without relying solely on one translation.

**Translators and philologists:** For clarifying etymology and finding Sanskrit parallels.

**How to use the dictionary?**

Information is organized into blocks:

**ET (Etymology):** Helps understand the meaning of a word through its roots.

**DEF (Definition):** A concise definition of the term.

**CTX (Contexts):** Shows the situations in which this concept was used.

**QUO (Quote):** Direct speech from the Canon, giving a living example of usage.

**REL (Related terms):** Links to other entries. It is recommended to follow them to see the entire "map" of the teaching.

**STG (Stage of path):** Indicates at which stage (virtue, concentration, or wisdom) this term is relevant.

**PRC (Practice significance):** Advice on how to transform understanding of the term into personal experience.

May this knowledge be for the benefit of all beings!

# BUDDHIST DICTIONARY OF THE PĀLI TRADITION

*Based on suttas of the Pāli Canon (Dīgha-nikāya — DN, Majjhima-nikāya — MN, Saṃyutta-nikāya — SN, Aṅguttara-nikāya — AN)*

---

## 1. Basic Categories

---

<a name="term-1"></a>
## 1. Abhijjhā

**Skt.: abhidhyā**

**ET:** abhi- (intensifying prefix, "toward") + *√jhā* (to think, contemplate) = "thinking toward," greedy gazing at an object of desire.

**DEF:** Longing to possess what belongs to another; directed desire for appropriation. A form of unwholesome intention (akusala saṅkappa), connected with lobha (greed) and taṇhā (craving). In the Canon, it is included in the list of mental defilements and is opposed to anabhijjhā (non-covetousness) — a factor of right view.

**CTX:**
1. **As an element of unwholesome intention** — in SN 45.8 (Magga-vibhaṅga), abhijjhā is part of the triad of unwholesome intentions along with vyāpāda (ill-will) and vihiṃsā (cruelty). Right intention (sammāsaṅkappa) is defined as freedom from these three.
2. **As a mental defilement** — in AN 5.193, it is included in the list of mental defilements that obscure clear vision.
3. **As a result of wrong attention** — arises from ayoniso manasikāra (AN 1.11–15), when the mind is improperly directed towards an attractive object.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is right intention? Intention of renunciation, intention of good-will, intention of harmlessness. This is called right intention." (SN 45.8)

**REL:**
- → Lobha (greed as root)
- → Vyāpāda (ill-will)
- → Sammāsaṅkappa (right intention)
- → Ayoniso manasikāra (improper attention)
- → Alobha (non-greed)

**STG:** Overcome at the initial stages of moral discipline (sīla) and further through the development of sammāsaṅkappa and samādhi.

**PRC:** Recognizing abhijjhā in the mind is part of satipaṭṭhāna practice (contemplation of dhammas). Its removal is linked to developing asubha-bhāvanā (contemplation of the unattractive body) and understanding anicca.

---

<a name="term-2"></a>
## 2. Abhisaṅkhāra

**Skt.: abhisaṃskāra**

**ET:** abhi- (intensifying prefix) + *saṃ-* (together) + *√kar* (to do) = "co-making," gathering of volitional constructs.

**DEF:** Volitional formations that produce kammic results and condition further existence. The second link (nidāna) in the chain of dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda), conditioned by ignorance (avijjā). Includes three types: bodily (kāya-saṅkhāra), verbal (vacī-saṅkhāra), and mental (citta-saṅkhāra) activities.

**CTX:**
1. **As a link of dependent origination** — in SN 12.2: "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations [arise]." They shape the further arising of consciousness (viññāṇa). The cessation of abhisaṅkhāra occurs with the removal of avijjā.
2. **As intentional constructs of experience** — in MN 44 (Cūḷavedalla Sutta), it is emphasized that these formations are volitional (sañcetanā) and create kammic potential for future existence.
3. **As differing in nature** — they are divided into meritorious (puñña), demeritorious (apuñña), and imperturbable (āneñja) formations, depending on the level of consciousness at which they arise.

**QUO:**
> "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations [arise]; with volitional formations as condition, consciousness [arises]... Such is the origin of this whole mass of suffering." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Avijjā (ignorance as condition)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness as result)
- → Kamma (kamma)
- → Cetanā (intention)
- → Saṅkhārakkhandha (aggregate of formations)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)

**STG:** The cessation of abhisaṅkhāra begins with the development of wisdom (paññā) and is completed at the level of arahant, when ignorance is completely destroyed and new kammic formations are no longer created.

**PRC:** Awareness that every intentional action of body, speech, and mind creates a kammic trace encourages the development of mindfulness (sati) and attention to one's motivations (cetanā).

---

<a name="term-3"></a>
## 3. Akusala

**Skt.: akuśala**

**ET:** a- (negative particle) + *kusala* (wholesome, skillful) = "unskillful," unwholesome.

**DEF:** Unwholesome (unskillful) states and actions that lead to suffering (dukkha) and unwholesome results. Opposed to kusala (wholesome). In MN 9 (Sammādiṭṭhi Sutta), it is systematically defined through the three unwholesome roots (akusala-mūla).

**CTX:**
1. **Through the three roots** — in MN 9, akusala is defined as states rooted in lobha (greed), dosa (hatred), and moha (delusion). Any unwholesome action, word, or thought proceeds from one or more of these roots.
2. **Opposition to kusala** — in AN 3.69 (Akusala-mūla Sutta), a systematic distinction is made: akusala leads to suffering and is born from wrong understanding, while kusala leads to happiness and is born from wisdom.
3. **As an object of practice** — practice is aimed at recognizing akusala at the moment of its arising and removing it through the development of wholesome opposites.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the unwholesome? Unwholesome bodily action, unwholesome verbal action, unwholesome mental action rooted in greed, hatred, and delusion." (MN 9)

**REL:**
- → Kusala (wholesome)
- → Lobha, Dosa, Moha (three roots)
- → Akusala-kamma (unwholesome action)
- → Sīla (moral discipline)
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)

**STG:** Recognizing akusala begins with the first stage — sammādiṭṭhi — and continues at all stages up to its complete eradication at the level of arahant.

**PRC:** The basic skill of a practitioner is to distinguish between wholesome and unwholesome in one's own mind. This discrimination (dhammavicaya) is one of the factors of awakening (bojjhaṅga).

---

<a name="term-4"></a>
## 4. Anattā

**Skt.: anātman**

**ET:** na (not) + *attā* (self, "I") = "non-self," absence of a permanent self.

**DEF:** Absence of a permanent, independent "self" or essence (attā) in all phenomena. One of the three characteristics of existence (tilakkhaṇa) along with anicca and dukkha. In SN 22.59 (Anattalakkhana Sutta) — the Buddha's second discourse after his enlightenment — the Buddha shows that all five aggregates (pañcakkhandha) are not "self."

**CTX:**
1. **Analysis of the five aggregates** — in SN 22.59, the Buddha systematically examines each of the five aggregates: "Form, bhikkhus, is not-self. If form were self, it would not lead to affliction..." — concluding that none of them can be considered "self."
2. **Through the six sense bases** — in MN 148 (Chachakka Sutta), anattā is revealed through the analysis of the six internal and external bases (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind and corresponding objects), showing that all experience is impersonal.
3. **As a result of direct vision** — anattā is understood not as a philosophical theory, but as a result of direct vision (yathābhūtañāṇadassana) of the nature of phenomena. Realization of this leads to nibbidā (disenchantment) and vimutti (liberation).

**QUO:**
> "Therefore, Susīma, any kind of form whatsoever — whether past, future, or present, internal or external, gross or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near — all form should be seen as it really is with correct wisdom: 'This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.'" (SN 22.59 / SN 12.70)

**REL:**
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Pañcakkhandha (five aggregates)
- → Attā (self)
- → Yathābhūtañāṇadassana (seeing things as they are)
- → Vimutti (liberation)

**STG:** Direct realization of anattā occurs at the level of sotāpatti (stream-entry), when sakkāya-diṭṭhi (personality view) is destroyed. Complete liberation from all forms of self-identification occurs at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Contemplation of anattā in everyday experience: observing that all phenomena (body, feelings, perceptions, thoughts, consciousness) arise and cease independently of anyone's control. This destroys attachment and self-identification.

---

<a name="term-5"></a>
## 5. Anicca

**Skt.: anitya**

**ET:** na (not) + *nicca* (constant, stable) = "impermanent."

**DEF:** Impermanence of all conditioned phenomena (saṅkhata dhamma). The first of the three characteristics of existence (tilakkhaṇa). In SN 22.59, it is revealed through the changing nature of the five aggregates: everything that arises (uppāda) is subject to disappearance (vaya) and alteration (vipariṇāma).

**CTX:**
1. **Through the five aggregates** — in SN 22.59, the Buddha shows that form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are impermanent because they change from moment to moment.
2. **As a basis for insight** — in MN 64 (Mahā Māluṅkya Sutta), understanding anicca leads to nibbidā (disenchantment) and virāga (dispassion), which in turn leads to liberation.
3. **Universality** — anicca encompasses all levels of experience, from material objects to subtle mental states. Even jhānas and formless attainments are impermanent.

**QUO:**
> "Form, bhikkhus, is impermanent. What is impermanent is suffering. What is suffering is not-self. What is not-self — this is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self." (SN 22.59)

**REL:**
- → Anattā (non-self)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Uppāda (arising)
- → Vaya (disappearance)
- → Vipariṇāma (change)
- → Tilakkhaṇa (three characteristics)

**STG:** Contemplation of anicca begins at the initial stages of vipassanā and deepens all the way to full liberation.

**PRC:** Observing anicca in the body, feelings, and thoughts in real time is the foundation of vipassanā practice. This destroys the illusion of stability (nicca-saññā) and reduces clinging.

---

<a name="term-6"></a>
## 6. Anusaya

**Skt.: anuśaya**

**ET:** anu- (along, according to) + *√śī* (to lie) = "lying along with," latent tendency, dormant inclination.

**DEF:** Latent, dormant tendencies of the mind that underlie reactions to objects of experience. They are not necessarily active at a given moment, but manifest under suitable conditions. In MN 64, it is said that anusaya manifest in the absence of mindfulness.

**CTX:**
1. **Seven latent tendencies** — the Canon distinguishes seven anusaya: kāma-rāga (sensual passion), paṭigha (irritation), diṭṭhi (view), vicikicchā (doubt), māna (conceit), bhava-rāga (passion for existence), avijjā (ignorance). They are listed in AN 7.11.
2. **Activation upon contact** — anusaya are activated (pariyuṭṭhāna) upon contact (phassa) with corresponding objects if mindfulness is absent. For example, upon contact with a pleasant object, the anusaya of kāma-rāga is activated.
3. **Eradication at stages of the path** — different anusaya are eradicated at different stages: diṭṭhi and vicikicchā at the level of sotāpatti; kāma-rāga and paṭigha at the level of anāgāmitā; māna, bhava-rāga, and avijjā at the level of arahatta.

**QUO:**
> "As long as ignorance has not been removed and knowledge has not arisen, the latent tendency to passion, the latent tendency to irritation, the latent tendency to the view of self... continue to lie [in the mind]." (MN 64)

**REL:**
- → Kilesa (defilements)
- → Pariyuṭṭhāna (active manifestation)
- → Vitikkama (transgression through action)
- → Ariya-sāvaka (noble disciple)
- → Sotāpatti (stream-entry)

**STG:** Three levels are distinguished: vitikkama (transgression through action) is removed through sīla; pariyuṭṭhāna (active manifestation) is removed through samādhi; anusaya (latent tendency) is eradicated through paññā.

**PRC:** Understanding anusaya helps the practitioner not to be satisfied with the temporary suppression of defilements, but to strive for their complete eradication through insight.

---

<a name="term-7"></a>
## 7. Arahant

**Skt.: arhat**

**ET:** from *√arh* (to be worthy, to deserve) = "worthy one," one who deserves veneration.

**DEF:** One who has completely destroyed all mental defilements (āsava-khaya), attained final liberation (vimutti), and no longer creates new kamma leading to rebirth. The highest goal of the Buddhist path.

**CTX:**
1. **Non-identification with the aggregates** — in SN 22.59, the arahant is described as one who no longer identifies with form, feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness. He sees: "This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self."
2. **Absence of attachment** — in MN 1 (Mūlapariyāya Sutta), it is emphasized that the arahant is not attached even to the highest states, including nibbāna as an object of perception.
3. **Actions do not create kamma** — since ignorance (avijjā) is completely destroyed, the arahant's actions are simply kiriya (functional actions), not creating kammic results.

**QUO:**
> "Birth is destroyed, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more coming back to any state of being." (SN 22.59)

**REL:**
- → Āsava (taints)
- → Vimutti (liberation)
- → Arahattaphala (fruit of arahantship)
- → Asekha (one beyond training)
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna)

**STG:** The fourth and highest stage of awakening. Preceding stages: sotāpanna → sakadāgāmī → anāgāmī → arahant.

**PRC:** The arahant is a living example that liberation is achievable. His qualities inspire practitioners to follow the path to the end.

<a name="term-8"></a>
## 8. Āsava

**Skt.: āsrava**

**ET:** ā- (toward) + *√sru* (to flow) = "inflow," "effluent," that which flows out of the mind or flows into it.

**DEF:** Deep "inflows" or taints of the mind that sustain saṃsāra. In MN 2 (Sabbāsava Sutta), methods for their removal are described. Three main āsava: kāmāsava (sensual desire), bhavāsava (desire for existence), and avijjāsava (ignorance). Some sources add a fourth — diṭṭhāsava (wrong view).

**CTX:**
1. **Methods of removal** — in MN 2, seven methods of working with āsava are described: seeing (dassana), restraint (saṃvara), using (paṭisevana), enduring (adhivāsana), avoiding (parivajjana), removing (vinodana), and developing (bhāvanā).
2. **Connection with liberation** — the complete destruction of āsava (āsavānaṃ khaya) is synonymous with the state of arahant. In DN 2, the knowledge of the destruction of āsava is the third higher knowledge (tevijja).
3. **As the driving force of saṃsāra** — āsava sustain the continuity of existence. Their cessation is linked to the development of wisdom (paññā) and completes the cycle of rebirths.

**QUO:**
> "Bhikkhus, I will teach you the method for restraining all taints... Here, bhikkhus, taints that should be abandoned by seeing... by restraining... by using... by enduring... by avoiding... by removing... by developing." (MN 2)

**REL:**
- → Kilesa (defilements)
- → Āsavānaṃ khaya (destruction of taints)
- → Vijjā (knowledge)
- → Arahant (arahant)
- → Yoniso manasikāra (wise attention)

**STG:** The destruction of āsava is the mark of an arahant. At preceding stages, āsava are partially weakened and removed.

**PRC:** Understanding that different taints require different methods of work prevents applying a single approach to all situations (the main idea of MN 2).

<a name="term-9"></a>
## 9. Asaṅkhata

**Skt.: asaṃskṛta**

**ET:** a- (negative particle) + *saṅkhata* (conditioned, compounded, fabricated) = "unconditioned," unfabricated.

**DEF:** The unconditioned state, which does not arise from causes, is not subject to arising and ceasing. In SN 43 (Asaṅkhata-saṃyutta), it is synonymous with nibbāna. Opposed to saṅkhata (conditioned). Its realization is the goal of the Buddhist path.

**CTX:**
1. **As a synonym for nibbāna** — in SN 43, many synonyms of asaṅkhata are listed: cessation (nirodha), end (anta), unconditioned (asaṅkhata), supreme (anuttara), secure (khema), etc.
2. **Beyond time** — in AN 3.47 (Saṅkhata-lakkhaṇa Sutta), it is said that asaṅkhata does not have the three characteristics of the conditioned: arising, ceasing, and alteration.
3. **The path to the unconditioned** — in SN 43, the Buddha enumerates practices leading to asaṅkhata: satipaṭṭhāna, right efforts, bases of psychic power, spiritual faculties and powers, factors of awakening, the eightfold path.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the unconditioned? The destruction of lust, the destruction of hatred, the destruction of delusion. This is called the unconditioned." (SN 38.1)

**REL:**
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna)
- → Saṅkhata (conditioned)
- → Nirodha (cessation)
- → Anuttara (supreme)
- → Vimutti (liberation)

**STG:** Asaṅkhata is realized by direct knowledge at the level of arahant. The path to it is the development of satipaṭṭhāna and other factors leading to the unconditioned (SN 43).

**PRC:** The distinction between the conditioned (saṅkhata) and the unconditioned (asaṅkhata) is crucial for correct understanding of the goal. All practices are conditioned (the path), but they lead to the unconditioned (the fruit).

<a name="term-10"></a>
## 10. Avijjā

**Skt.: avidyā**

**ET:** a- (negative particle) + *vijjā* (knowledge) = "non-knowledge," absence of direct understanding.

**DEF:** Fundamental ignorance, not understanding the four noble truths (cattāri ariyasaccāni). The first link (nidāna) in the chain of dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda). This is not merely a lack of information, but an active distorted perception of reality from which all conditioned processes arise.

**CTX:**
1. **As the first link of dependent origination** — in SN 12.2, avijjā sets the entire cycle of saṃsāra in motion: "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations (saṅkhārā) [arise]." It is defined as not knowing the four noble truths.
2. **As an unwholesome root (akusala-mūla)** — in the form of moha (delusion), avijjā is one of the three unwholesome roots alongside lobha and dosa (MN 9).
3. **As that which is removed by knowledge** — in DN 2, vijjā is described as the result of complete practice of the path, including three higher knowledges: knowledge of past lives (pubbe-nivāsānussati-ñāṇa), knowledge of death and rebirth of beings (cutūpapāta-ñāṇa), and knowledge of the destruction of taints (āsavānaṃ khaya-ñāṇa).

**QUO:**
> "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations [arise]; with volitional formations as condition, consciousness [arises]... Such is the origin of this whole mass of suffering." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Vijjā (knowledge)
- → Moha (delusion)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)
- → Āsava (taints)
- → Nibbāna (cessation of avijjā)

**STG:** Overcoming avijjā begins with sammādiṭṭhi at the initial stage and is completed with its full destruction at the level of arahant.

**PRC:** Avijjā is overcome not by accumulating information, but by direct vision (yathābhūtañāṇadassana) of the nature of phenomena through the development of satipaṭṭhāna and the realization of the three characteristics (anicca, dukkha, anattā).

## 11. Bhava

**Skt.: bhava**

**ET:** from *√bhū* (to be, to become) = "becoming," the process of existence.

**DEF:** Becoming or the process of existence — the tenth link of dependent origination. In SN 12.2, it is the link leading to birth (jāti). There are three types of bhava: kāma-bhava (sense-sphere becoming), rūpa-bhava (form-sphere becoming), and arūpa-bhava (formless-sphere becoming). It is sustained by clinging (upādāna).

**CTX:**
1. **As a link of dependent origination** — in SN 12.2: "With clinging as condition, becoming; with becoming as condition, birth." Bhava is the kammic potential that leads to new existence.
2. **Three types of becoming** — kāma-bhava (connected with sense worlds), rūpa-bhava (connected with jhānas of form), arūpa-bhava (connected with formless attainments). All three are part of saṃsāra (SN 12.66).
3. **Cessation** — bhava ceases with the removal of upādāna (clinging), which occurs through full understanding of the nature of experience. Its cessation leads to liberation from birth (MN 38).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is becoming? There are three types of becoming: sense-sphere becoming, form-sphere becoming, formless-sphere becoming. This is called becoming." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Upādāna (clinging)
- → Jāti (birth)
- → Kamma (kamma)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Kāmaloka / Rūpaloka / Arūpaloka (three worlds)

**STG:** Bhava as a process completely ceases at the level of arahant, when all kammic potential for new existence is exhausted.

**PRC:** Understanding bhava shows that even subtle forms of existence (including heavenly worlds and formless states) remain part of saṃsāra. This motivates one to strive not for better rebirths, but for complete liberation.

---

<a name="term-12"></a>
## 12. Bhikkhu

**Skt.: bhikṣu**

**ET:** from *√bhikṣ* (to beg, to live on alms) = "beggar," one who lives on alms.

**DEF:** A monk in the Buddhist community (saṅgha), who has left household life to practice the path of liberation. In DN 2 (Sāmaññaphala Sutta), his way of life is described: he follows moral discipline (sīla), guards the sense doors, develops mindfulness and satipaṭṭhāna, attains jhānas, and finally destroys the taints.

**CTX:**
1. **As the ideal practitioner** — in DN 2, King Ajātasattu asks about the fruits of the contemplative life, and the Buddha describes the sequential path of a bhikkhu from morality to liberation.
2. **Four supports** — a bhikkhu is content with any robe, any alms food, any dwelling, any medicine. This simplicity supports practice.
3. **As a recipient of the teaching** — most suttas are addressed to bhikkhus, and the Buddha often addresses them as his primary audience.

**QUO:**
> "And how, great king, does a monk become accomplished in morality? Abandoning the destruction of life, he refrains from destroying life..." (DN 2)

**REL:**
- → Bhikkhunī (nun)
- → Saṅgha (community)
- → Pabbajjā (going forth)
- → Sīla (morality)
- → Piṇḍapāta (alms round)

**STG:** Monasticism creates optimal conditions for practice but does not guarantee liberation. Many bhikkhus attain the higher stages, but lay followers (upāsaka) can also reach sotāpatti and beyond.

**PRC:** The way of life of a bhikkhu is a model of complete dedication to practice, based on simplicity, morality, and continuous development of the mind.

---

<a name="term-13"></a>
## 13. Bhikkhunī

**Skt.: bhikṣuṇī**

**ET:** feminine form of *bhikkhu* (monk) = "nun."

**DEF:** A nun in the Buddhist community (saṅgha). She follows the same principles as a bhikkhu. Her path is directed towards liberation. In AN 8.51, her qualities and practice are described. The eightfold path is the same for both sexes.

**CTX:**
1. **Practice** — a bhikkhunī follows the same discipline as a bhikkhu, with additional rules (garudhamma) ensuring order in the community (AN 8.51).
2. **Capacity for liberation** — in SN 5 (Bhikkhunī-saṃyutta), it is shown that nuns are as capable of attaining the higher stages as monks. Māra tries to hinder them, but unsuccessfully.

**QUO:**
> Māra says: "That state which is hard to attain, which should be attained by the seer, a woman cannot achieve it — her wisdom is only two fingers' worth." The nun replies: "When the mind is well concentrated, when knowledge has arisen, then whoever thinks 'I am a woman' or 'I am a man' — he is fit to speak to Māra." (SN 5.2)

**REL:**
- → Bhikkhu (monk)
- → Saṅgha (community)
- → Garudhamma (eight heavy rules)
- → Upāsikā (laywoman)

**STG:** A bhikkhunī can attain all four stages of awakening, including arahatta. Gender is not an obstacle to liberation.

**PRC:** The example of bhikkhunīs shows that the path to liberation is open to all, regardless of gender. This refutes any discriminatory notions about spiritual capacities.

---

<a name="term-14"></a>
## 14. Bodhi

**Skt.: bodhi**

**ET:** from *√budh* (to awaken, to understand) = "awakening."

**DEF:** Awakening, full understanding of reality, attained at the level of a Buddha. In Ud 1.1 (the first verse after enlightenment), it is linked to the realization of dependent origination. This is the state of complete liberation from ignorance (avijjā).

**CTX:**
1. **Under the Bodhi tree** — in MN 26 (Ariyapariyesanā Sutta), the Buddha's search and awakening after long effort are described.
2. **Three types of bodhi** — tradition distinguishes: sammā-sambodhi (full awakening of a Buddha), pacceka-bodhi (awakening of a "silent Buddha"), and sāvaka-bodhi (awakening of an arahant disciple).

**QUO:**
> "When, bhikkhus, such things were realized by me... knowledge and vision arose: unshakable is my liberation, this is my last birth, there is no more coming back to any state of being." (Ud 1.1)

**REL:**
- → Buddha (Buddha)
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna)
- → Vijjā (knowledge)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)

**STG:** Bodhi in the full sense is attained only by a Buddha. Arahants attain sāvaka-bodhi (the awakening of a disciple).

**PRC:** Bodhi is not merely intellectual understanding, but a complete transformation of perception, attained through the development of all aspects of the path.

---

<a name="term-15"></a>
## 15. Buddha

**Skt.: buddha**

**ET:** past participle of *√budh* (to awaken) = "awakened one."

**DEF:** The Awakened One, one who has independently discovered the path to liberation and teaches it to others. In DN 16 (Mahāparinibbāna Sutta), his last days are described. He is the teacher of gods and humans (satthā devamanussānaṃ). His teaching is directed towards the cessation of suffering.

**CTX:**
1. **As teacher** — in DN 16, the Buddha before parinibbāna instructs the monks: "Be islands unto yourselves (attadīpā viharatha)."
2. **Nine qualities of the Buddha** — often enumerated in the Canon: iti'pi so bhagavā arahaṃ sammāsambuddho vijjācaraṇasampanno sugato lokavidū anuttaro purisadammasārathi satthā devamanussānaṃ buddho bhagavā.
3. **A human, not a god** — the Buddha emphasized that he was a human being who attained awakening through his own efforts.

**QUO:**
> "All compounded things decay. Strive diligently for the goal." — The Buddha's last words (DN 16)

**REL:**
- → Bodhi (awakening)
- → Dhamma (teaching)
- → Saṅgha (community)
- → Tathāgata (epithet)
- → Sammāsambuddha (fully awakened one)

**STG:** Becoming a Buddha is the result of developing ten perfections (pāramī) over countless lives. Practitioners may strive for arahatta, not for Buddhahood.

**PRC:** The Buddha is not an object of worship, but an example that liberation is achievable through human effort. His life inspires practice.

---

<a name="term-16"></a>
## 16. Cetanā

**Skt.: cetanā**

**ET:** from *√cit* (to perceive, to intend) = "intention," volitional impulse.

**DEF:** Intention or will, which is defined as kamma in AN 6.63 (Nibbedhika Sutta). This is the key factor determining the kammic quality of actions. It directs body, speech, and mind. Kammic results depend on the quality of intention.

**CTX:**
1. **Intention as kamma** — in AN 6.63: "Intention, bhikkhus, is kamma. Having intended, one performs an action by body, speech, or mind."
2. **Connection with formations** — in SN 12.38, cetanā is linked to the formation of saṅkhāra. When one intends, plans, or has latent tendencies (anusaya), this becomes a support for consciousness.

**QUO:**
> "Intention, bhikkhus, is kamma. Having intended, one performs an action by body, speech, or mind." (AN 6.63)

**REL:**
- → Kamma (kamma)
- → Sañcetanā (co-intention)
- → Saṅkhāra (formations)
- → Citta (mind)

**PRC:** Mindfulness (sati) allows one to observe intentions before they manifest as actions, providing the opportunity for choice and transformation of unwholesome tendencies.

---

<a name="term-17"></a>
## 17. Citta

**Skt.: citta**

**ET:** from *√cit* (to perceive, to know) = "mind," "consciousness," "state of mind."

**DEF:** Mind as a stream of conscious states. In SN 1.2, it is described as changeable and subject to influence. It can be defiled (saṅkiliṭṭha) or purified (parisuddha). The central role in practice — liberation is connected with the purification of citta.

**CTX:**
1. **Changeability** — in SN 1.2, a deity asks: "What is the fastest thing?" The Buddha answers: "The mind (citta) — it is the fastest."
2. **As a field of practice** — in MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta), citta is the third foundation of mindfulness (cittānupassanā): the practitioner observes states of mind — with passion, without passion, with hatred, without hatred, etc.
3. **Purification** — in AN 1.1–10 it is said: "I do not see any other thing that so defiles the mind as greed... I do not see any other thing that so purifies the mind as wisdom."

**QUO:**
> "Luminous, bhikkhus, is this mind. But it is defiled by adventitious defilements." (AN 1.51)

**REL:**
- → Cetanā (intention)
- → Mano (mind as faculty)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Cittānupassanā (contemplation of mind)
- → Samādhi (concentration)

**PRC:** The practice of satipaṭṭhāna includes direct observation of states of mind (cittānupassanā), allowing one to see defilements at the moment of their arising and to develop wholesome states.

---

<a name="term-18"></a>
## 18. Dhamma

**Skt.: dharma**

**ET:** from *√dhar* (to hold, to support) = "that which holds"; law, teaching, phenomenon.

**DEF:** A multi-faceted term: (1) the Buddha's teaching, (2) phenomena as such, (3) the law of reality, (4) a quality or property. In DN 1 (Brahmajāla Sutta), Dhamma is contrasted with wrong views. It is both the path and the truth simultaneously.

**CTX:**
1. **As teaching** — in DN 16, the Buddha says: "The Dhamma and Vinaya that I have proclaimed will be your teacher after my passing."
2. **As phenomena** — in MN 1 (Mūlapariyāya Sutta), dhamma refers to all objects of perception, including forms, feelings, perceptions, etc.
3. **As the fourth foundation of mindfulness** — dhammānupassanā (contemplation of dhammas) includes observation of the five hindrances, five aggregates, six sense bases, seven factors of awakening, and four noble truths (MN 10).
4. **As the law of causality** — paṭiccasamuppāda is a concrete manifestation of Dhamma as law.

**QUO:**
> "Who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma; who sees the Dhamma sees dependent origination." (MN 28)

**REL:**
- → Vinaya (discipline)
- → Sutta (discourse)
- → Dhammānupassanā (contemplation of dhammas)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Saṅgha (community)

**STG:** Understanding of Dhamma deepens at each stage, from theoretical knowledge to direct realization.

**PRC:** Dhamma is both the teaching to be studied and the reality to be directly realized. Study without practice is incomplete; practice without study may be misguided.

---

<a name="term-19"></a>
## 19. Dukkha

**Skt.: duḥkha**

**ET:** du- (bad, difficult) + *kha* (axle, space) = "badly rotating wheel," that which is hard to bear.

**DEF:** Suffering or unsatisfactoriness of all conditioned phenomena. The first noble truth (ariya-sacca). In SN 56.11 (Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta), the Buddha defines dukkha as birth, aging, illness, death, union with the unpleasant, separation from the pleasant, not getting what one wants — and in brief, the five aggregates of clinging (pañcupādānakkhandha) are dukkha.

**CTX:**
1. **Three types of dukkha** — dukkha-dukkhatā (ordinary suffering), vipariṇāma-dukkhatā (suffering due to change), saṅkhāra-dukkhatā (suffering due to the conditioned nature of all phenomena).
2. **The noble truth** — in SN 56.11, the Buddha proclaims: "This is the noble truth of suffering." He further explains that this truth must be fully understood (pariññeyya).
3. **Cause and cessation** — dukkha has a cause (taṇhā) and a cessation (nirodha). The fourth truth points to the path leading to cessation.

**QUO:**
> "Birth is suffering, aging is suffering, illness is suffering, death is suffering, sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair are suffering; not getting what one wants is suffering; in brief, the five aggregates subject to clinging are suffering." (SN 56.11)

**REL:**
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Anattā (non-self)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Ariya-sacca (noble truth)
- → Nibbāna (cessation of suffering)

**STG:** Dukkha is fully understood at the level of sotāpatti, but completely overcome only at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Correct understanding of dukkha is not pessimism, but realistic vision. It motivates practice and directs efforts towards true liberation.

---

<a name="term-20"></a>
## 20. Nibbāna

**Skt.: nirvāṇa**

**ET:** ni- (negative particle, "without") + *√vā* (to blow) = "extinguishing," "going out" (like the extinguishing of a flame).

**DEF:** The cessation of suffering and liberation from saṃsāra. In SN 43.14, it is described as the unconditioned (asaṅkhata). It is defined as the complete extinguishing of lust (rāga), hatred (dosa), and delusion (moha). It is not a place or a state in the ordinary sense. It is the final goal of the path.

**CTX:**
1. **As extinguishing the three roots** — in SN 38.1: "Nibbāna is the complete extinguishing of lust, the complete extinguishing of hatred, the complete extinguishing of delusion."
2. **Two aspects of nibbāna** — sa-upādisesa-nibbāna (nibbāna with residue — during the arahant's life) and anupādisesa-nibbāna (nibbāna without residue — after the arahant's death, parinibbāna).
3. **As unconditioned** — in SN 43, the Buddha enumerates 44 synonyms of nibbāna, including: asaṅkhata (unconditioned), anta (end), anāsava (without taints), sacca (truth), pāra (the far shore), sududdasa (hard to see), ajara (ageless), dhuva (stable), apalokita (unfailing).

**QUO:**
> "There is, bhikkhus, an unborn, unbecome, unmade, unconditioned. If there were not this unborn... there would be no escape from the born, become, made, conditioned." (Ud 8.3)

**REL:**
- → Asaṅkhata (unconditioned)
- → Vimutti (liberation)
- → Arahant (arahant)
- → Rāga, Dosa, Moha (three roots)
- → Nirodha (cessation)

**STG:** Nibbāna is realized in four stages: sotāpatti (partial realization), sakadāgāmitā, anāgāmitā, arahatta (full realization).

**PRC:** Nibbāna is not nihilistic annihilation, but the realization of what is already present when defilements are removed. Practice gradually approaches this realization.

---

<a name="term-21"></a>
## 21. Vimutti

**Skt.: vimukti**

**ET:** vi- (separation, intensification) + *√muc* (to release) = "liberation."

**DEF:** Liberation of the mind from defilements. In SN 43.14, it is linked to the attainment of nibbāna. Two types are distinguished: cetovimutti (liberation of mind through samādhi) and paññāvimutti (liberation through wisdom). Full liberation corresponds to the state of arahant.

**CTX:**
1. **Two types** — in AN 2.29 and MN 43, they are distinguished: cetovimutti (liberation of mind from passion through concentration) and paññāvimutti (liberation from ignorance through wisdom). Full liberation includes both.
2. **As a result of practice** — in SN 22.59: "Seeing thus... he becomes disenchanted. Through disenchantment [his mind] is liberated. When it is liberated, the knowledge comes: 'Liberated.'"
3. **Temporary and final** — temporary liberation is attained through jhānas (suppression of hindrances); final liberation through complete destruction of defilements (arahatta).

**QUO:**
> "When it is liberated, the knowledge comes: 'Liberated.' He understands: 'Birth is destroyed, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more coming back to any state of being.'" (SN 22.59)

**REL:**
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna)
- → Arahant (arahant)
- → Cetovimutti (liberation of mind)
- → Paññāvimutti (liberation through wisdom)
- → Āsavānaṃ khaya (destruction of taints)

**STG:** Temporary liberation is accessible through jhānas at any stage. Complete and irreversible liberation only at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Distinguishing between temporary and final liberation is important, so as not to be satisfied with temporary attainments (e.g., jhānas), but to strive for the complete eradication of defilements.

---

<a name="term-22"></a>
## 22. Dosa

**Skt.: dveṣa**

**ET:** from *√duṣ* (to spoil, to be dissatisfied) = "hatred," "aversion," "ill-will."

**DEF:** Hatred, aversion, ill-will — the second of the three unwholesome roots (akusala-mūla). In MN 9, it is defined as a state of mind directed towards harming others or repelling objects. Opposed to alobha (non-greed) and adosa (non-hatred, mettā). Together with lobha and moha, it forms the three roots of all unwholesome actions.

**CTX:**
1. **As an unwholesome root** — in MN 9: "When hatred arises, what does it produce? It produces unwholesome bodily, verbal, and mental actions."
2. **As that which is removed by mettā** — in SN 46.54, mettā-bhāvanā (development of loving-kindness) is the direct antidote to dosa.
3. **In a pair with lobha** — dosa and lobha are two opposite but equally unwholesome reactions: attraction (lobha) and repulsion (dosa).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the unwholesome root? Greed (lobha) is an unwholesome root, hatred (dosa) is an unwholesome root, delusion (moha) is an unwholesome root." (MN 9)

**REL:**
- → Lobha (greed)
- → Moha (delusion)
- → Akusala-mūla (unwholesome roots)
- → Adosa (non-hatred)
- → Mettā (loving-kindness)
- → Vyāpāda (ill-will)

**STG:** Dosa is completely eradicated at the level of anāgāmitā (non-returner). It is partially weakened at the level of sakadāgāmitā.

**PRC:** Observing dosa in the mind allows one to see how aversion creates suffering. The practice of mettā-bhāvanā is a direct method for working with dosa.

---

<a name="term-23"></a>
## 23. Lobha

**Skt.: lobha**

**ET:** from *√lubh* (to crave, to desire) = "greed," "passionate desire."

**DEF:** Greed, passionate desire, attachment — the first of the three unwholesome roots (akusala-mūla). In MN 9, it is defined as a state of mind directed towards appropriation and retention of objects. Opposed to alobha (non-greed, generosity). It is the basis for abhijjhā (covetousness) and kāmacchanda (sensual desire).

**CTX:**
1. **As an unwholesome root** — in MN 9, lobha produces unwholesome actions of body, speech, and mind.
2. **Connection with taṇhā** — lobha is a broader concept than taṇhā (craving). Taṇhā is a specific form of lobha directed towards sensual pleasures, existence, or non-existence.
3. **Removal** — lobha is removed through contemplation of anicca (impermanence), asubha (unattractiveness), and the development of dāna (generosity).

**QUO:**
> "Greed, bhikkhus, is unwholesome. And whatever arises from greed — all that is unwholesome." (MN 9)

**REL:**
- → Dosa (hatred)
- → Moha (delusion)
- → Akusala-mūla (unwholesome roots)
- → Alobha (non-greed)
- → Dāna (generosity)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Rāga (passion)

**STG:** Lobha in the form of sensual passion (kāma-rāga) is completely eradicated at the level of anāgāmitā. More subtle forms (bhava-rāga) are eradicated at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Recognizing lobha in everyday life is a key skill. Countermeasures: contemplation of impermanence, developing generosity, and practicing asubha-bhāvanā.

---

<a name="term-24"></a>
## 24. Moha

**Skt.: moha**

**ET:** from *√muh* (to be deluded, to be bewildered) = "delusion," "blindness."

**DEF:** Delusion, mental blindness, inability to see reality as it is — the third of the three unwholesome roots (akusala-mūla). In MN 9, moha is defined as not knowing the four noble truths. It is the most fundamental of the three roots, since lobha and dosa are always accompanied by moha. In essence, moha is the same as avijjā (ignorance).

**CTX:**
1. **As an unwholesome root** — in MN 9: "When delusion arises, what does it produce? It produces unwholesome bodily, verbal, and mental actions."
2. **As the deepest root** — without moha, lobha and dosa cannot arise. It is moha that underlies the incorrect perception of reality.
3. **Removal** — moha is removed only through the development of paññā (wisdom) and direct vision (yathābhūtañāṇadassana).

**QUO:**
> "Delusion, bhikkhus, is unwholesome. And whatever arises from delusion — all that is unwholesome." (MN 9)

**REL:**
- → Lobha (greed)
- → Dosa (hatred)
- → Akusala-mūla (unwholesome roots)
- → Avijjā (ignorance)
- → Amoha (non-delusion)
- → Paññā (wisdom)
- → Vijjā (knowledge)

**STG:** Moha is completely eradicated only at the level of arahatta. It is partially weakened at the level of sotāpatti when sakkāya-diṭṭhi is destroyed.

**PRC:** Moha is the most difficult root to recognize, since it itself obscures the ability to see. Developing sati and vipassanā is the only way to its removal.

---

<a name="term-25"></a>
## 25. Kusala

**Skt.: kuśala**

**ET:** from *√kuś* (to be skillful, adept) = "skillful," "wholesome," "good."

**DEF:** Wholesome, skillful, good states and actions that lead to happiness and beneficial results. Opposed to akusala (unwholesome). In MN 9 (Sammādiṭṭhi Sutta), it is systematically defined through the three wholesome roots (kusala-mūla): alobha (non-greed), adosa (non-hatred), and amoha (non-delusion). Wholesome actions bring happy results and lead to progress on the path.

**CTX:**
1. **Through the three roots** — in MN 9, kusala is defined as states rooted in alobha (non-greed), adosa (non-hatred), and amoha (non-delusion). Any wholesome action proceeds from these roots.
2. **Contrast with akusala** — in AN 3.69 (Akusala-mūla Sutta), a systematic distinction is made: akusala leads to suffering, kusala leads to happiness; akusala arises from wrong understanding, kusala from wisdom.
3. **Ten wholesome actions** — in the Canon (MN 41, AN 10.176), ten courses of wholesome actions (kusala-kamma-patha) are listed: three bodily (abstaining from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct), four verbal (abstaining from lying, slander, harsh speech, idle chatter), and three mental (non-covetousness, non-ill-will, right view).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is wholesome? Wholesome bodily action, wholesome verbal action, wholesome mental action rooted in non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion." (MN 9)

**REL:**
- → Akusala (unwholesome)
- → Alobha, Adosa, Amoha (three wholesome roots)
- → Kusala-kamma-patha (courses of wholesome kamma)
- → Sīla (morality)
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)

**STG:** The development of kusala begins with the first stage — sammādiṭṭhi — and is cultivated at all stages up to arahatta, when wholesome states become spontaneous and natural.

**PRC:** Distinguishing between kusala and akusala is a basic skill of the practitioner. This discrimination (dhammavicaya) is one of the factors of awakening (bojjhaṅga). Wholesome states are not just "good" — they are a support for further development of the path.

---

<a name="term-26"></a>
## 26. Kilesa

**Skt.: kleśa**

**ET:** from *√klis* (to be defiled, to suffer) = "defilement," "impurity," "stain of mind."

**DEF:** Defilements or impurities of the mind that make it impure and hinder liberation. In the Canon (SN 45.165), ten kilesa are enumerated: lobha (greed), dosa (hatred), moha (delusion), māna (conceit), diṭṭhi (wrong view), vicikicchā (doubt), thina (sloth), uddhacca (restlessness), ahirikā (lack of shame), anottappa (lack of moral dread). Kilesa are closely related to āsava and anusaya, but represent active manifestations of defilements.

**CTX:**
1. **As that which is purified** — in AN 1.51–52: "Luminous, bhikkhus, is this mind. But it is defiled by adventitious defilements (āgantukā kilesā)." Kilesa are described as "adventitious" — they are not an inherent part of the mind, but arise from wrong attention.
2. **Three levels** — kilesa manifest on three levels: anusaya (latent tendency), pariyuṭṭhāna (active manifestation), vitikkama (transgression through action). The path works with all three levels sequentially.

**QUO:**
> "Luminous, bhikkhus, is this mind. But it is defiled by adventitious defilements." (AN 1.51)

**REL:**
- → Āsava (taints)
- → Anusaya (latent tendencies)
- → Saṃyojana (fetters)
- → Pariyuṭṭhāna (active manifestation)
- → Visuddhi (purification)

**STG:** Kilesa are removed sequentially: gross ones through sīla, intermediate ones through samādhi, subtle ones through paññā. Full purification at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Understanding kilesa as "adventitious" (āgantukā) defilements, not as an inherent part of the mind, gives confidence in the possibility of complete purification. Satipaṭṭhāna practice allows recognizing them at the moment of arising.

---

<a name="term-27"></a>
## 27. Micchādiṭṭhi

**Skt.: mithyādṛṣṭi**

**ET:** micchā (falsely, wrongly) + *diṭṭhi* (view, opinion) = "wrong view."

**DEF:** Wrong view, false understanding of reality. The first factor of the wrong path (micchā-magga). In MN 9 (Sammādiṭṭhi Sutta), it is defined as a view that denies the foundations of morality and causality: "There is no giving, no offering, no sacrifice; no fruit or result of good and bad actions; no this world, no other world..." Opposed to sammādiṭṭhi (right view).

**CTX:**
1. **As denial of kamma** — in MN 117 (Mahācattārīsaka Sutta), micchādiṭṭhi is defined as a view that denies the causal relationship between actions and their results.
2. **As the view of self** — sakkāya-diṭṭhi (personality view) is a form of micchādiṭṭhi. It is the identification of the five aggregates with "self" or "mine" (SN 22.81).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is wrong view? 'There is no giving, no offering, no sacrifice; no fruit or result of good and bad actions; no this world, no other world...' — this, bhikkhus, is called wrong view." (MN 117)

**REL:**
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)
- → Sakkāya-diṭṭhi (personality view)
- → Diṭṭhi (view)
- → Micchā-magga (wrong path)
- → Sotāpatti (stream-entry)

**STG:** Micchādiṭṭhi in the form of sakkāya-diṭṭhi is destroyed at the level of sotāpatti. Other forms of wrong views are removed at various stages.

**PRC:** The first step on the path is replacing micchādiṭṭhi with sammādiṭṭhi. Without correct understanding of the nature of reality, all other efforts will be misdirected.

---

<a name="term-28"></a>
## 28. Nimitta

**Skt.: nimitta**

**ET:** from *√mi* (to establish, to measure) = "sign," "mark," "image," "appearance."

**DEF:** A sign or characteristic of an object of perception. In the context of practice — a mental image or characteristic that the mind grasps when perceiving an object. In the context of meditation — an object of concentration (e.g., uggaha-nimitta — the acquired sign in ānāpānasati practice). The mind grasps nimitta and based on it creates perception (saññā) and concepts.

**CTX:**
1. **As what the mind follows** — in MN 10: "He does not follow the sign (nimitta) of forms..." — meaning the practitioner does not allow the mind to get carried away by the characteristics of objects.
2. **As the basis of wrong attention** — in AN 1.11–15, ayoniso manasikāra (wrong attention) is defined as attention to the nimitta of an attractive object, which leads to the arising of abhijjhā.
3. **In meditation** — in samatha practice, three types are distinguished: parikamma-nimitta (preparatory sign), uggaha-nimitta (acquired sign), and paṭibhāga-nimitta (counterpart sign).

**QUO:**
> "He does not follow the sign (nimitta) of forms, the details (anuvyañjana) of forms." (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Ayoniso manasikāra (wrong attention)
- → Yoniso manasikāra (wise attention)
- → Saññā (perception)
- → Ārammaṇa (object)
- → Anuvyañjana (detailed sign)

**STG:** Working with nimitta is part of satipaṭṭhāna practice at all stages. The ability not to follow signs of objects develops with progress on the path.

**PRC:** Controlling attention to nimitta is the key to preventing the arising of unwholesome states. If the mind does not grasp at attractive signs of an object, abhijjhā does not arise.

<a name="term-29"></a>
## 29. Mano

**Skt.: manas**

**ET:** from *√man* (to think, to understand) = "mind" as the sixth sense faculty.

**DEF:** Mind as the sixth internal sense faculty (āyatana), corresponding to mental objects (dhammā). In the scheme of six bases (saḷāyatana), mano is the internal base (ajjhattikāyatana) along with cakkhu, sota, ghāna, jivhā, kāya. Its objects are dhammā (mental phenomena). Not to be confused with citta (mind as a stream of states) or viññāṇa (consciousness).

**CTX:**
1. **As the sixth base** — in MN 148 (Chachakka Sutta), mano is the internal base, in dependence on which and the corresponding dhammā, mano-viññāṇa (mental consciousness) arises.
2. **In the scheme of dependent origination** — in SN 12.2, saḷāyatana (six bases) includes mano. Contact (phassa) arises from the meeting of mano, dhammā, and mano-viññāṇa.
3. **Distinction of mano, citta and viññāṇa** — in MN 43 (Mahāvedalla Sutta), these three terms are distinguished: mano is the faculty, citta is the state of mind, viññāṇa is consciousness arising from contact of the faculty with the object.

**QUO:**
> "In dependence on the mind (mano) and mental objects (dhammā) arises mental consciousness (mano-viññāṇa). The meeting of these three is contact (phassa)." (MN 148)

**REL:**
- → Citta (mind as state)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Āyatana (base)
- → Dhammā (mental objects)
- → Mano-viññāṇa (mental consciousness)
- → Saḷāyatana (six bases)

**STG:** Understanding mano as an organ, not a "self," contributes to the destruction of sakkāya-diṭṭhi. Contemplation of the six bases is part of dhammānupassanā (MN 10).

**PRC:** Distinguishing mano, citta, and viññāṇa helps accurately understand one's experience. When a thought or emotion arises, one can observe: there is the faculty (mano), there is the object (dhamma), there is consciousness (mano-viññāṇa). None of them is "I."

<a name="term-30"></a>
## 30. Assāda / Ādīnava / Nissaraṇa

**Skt.: āsvāda / ādīnava / niḥsaraṇa**

**ET:** assāda from ā- (toward) + *√svad* (to taste) = "taste," "enjoyment"; ādīnava from ā- + *√dī* (to be dissatisfied) = "danger," "drawback"; nissaraṇa from nis- (out, away) + *√sṛ* (to flow) = "escape," "release."

**DEF:** A threefold analysis of any sphere of experience: (1) assāda — the enjoyment, gratification, pleasant aspect; (2) ādīnava — the danger, drawback, unsatisfactory aspect; (3) nissaraṇa — the escape, release, liberation from it. In MN 13 (Mahādukkhakkhandha Sutta), the Buddha applies this triad to sensual pleasures, material form, and feelings. Complete understanding of all three aspects leads to liberation.

**CTX:**
1. **The triad as a method of analysis** — in MN 13, the Buddha examines sensual pleasures, form, and feelings through these three aspects, showing their attractiveness, their danger, and the way to transcend them.
2. **Applied to feelings** — the same triad is applied to the three types of feeling in SN 36.30: pleasant feelings are attractive (assāda), but impermanent and subject to change (ādīnava); the escape from them (nissaraṇa) is the removal of desire and passion for them.

**QUO:**
> "What, bhikkhus, is the gratification (assāda) of sensual pleasures? Sensual pleasures bring happiness and joy — this is their gratification. What is their danger? Sensual pleasures are impermanent, unsatisfactory, subject to change — this is their danger. What is the escape? The removal of desire and passion for sensual pleasures — this is the escape." (MN 13)

**REL:**
- → Assāda (gratification)
- → Ādīnava (danger)
- → Nissaraṇa (escape)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna as the ultimate escape)

**STG:** Understanding all three aspects deepens at each stage. The ultimate nissaraṇa corresponds to nibbāna, realized at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** This triad provides a complete analysis of any object of attachment: acknowledging its attractiveness, seeing its unsatisfactory nature, and finding the way to non-attachment. This is a structured approach to working with craving.

---

<a name="term-31"></a>
## 31. Khandha

**Skt.: skandha**

**ET:** from *√skandh* (to heap, to pile up) = "group," "aggregate," "heap."

**DEF:** Group, aggregate, collection of phenomena. In SN 22.48 (Khandha Sutta), five aggregates are enumerated: rūpa (form), vedanā (feeling), saññā (perception), saṅkhārā (formations), viññāṇa (consciousness). Together they constitute the totality of conditioned experience. They are not "self" — they are simply natural processes that arise and cease according to conditions.

**CTX:**
1. **Five aggregates** — each aggregate encompasses all phenomena of its class: rūpa includes all material phenomena (earth, water, fire, air elements and derived forms); vedanā — all feelings (pleasant, unpleasant, neutral); saññā — all perceptions (including recognition and labeling); saṅkhārā — all volitional and mental formations; viññāṇa — consciousness of the six sense doors.
2. **Analysis for non-self** — in SN 22.59 (Anattalakkhana Sutta), the Buddha shows that none of the aggregates is self, and grasping at them leads to suffering.
3. **Clung-to aggregates** — in contrast to mere aggregates (khandha), there are the "aggregates of clinging" (upādānakkhandha), which refer to the same aggregates when they are objects of clinging (SN 22.48).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, are the aggregates? Whatever kind of form... feeling... perception... formations... consciousness there is — whether past, future, or present, internal or external, gross or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near — this is called the aggregate of form/feeling/perception/formations/consciousness." (SN 22.48)

**REL:**
- → Pañcakkhandha (five aggregates)
- → Rūpa (form)
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Saññā (perception)
- → Saṅkhāra (formation)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Upādānakkhandha (aggregates of clinging)

**STG:** Understanding the aggregates begins with theoretical study. Direct insight into their nature (anicca, dukkha, anattā) develops on the path and culminates at arahatta.

**PRC:** The teaching on khandha provides a framework for analyzing experience. Direct observation of the five aggregates in meditation shows that what we call "self" is merely a collection of ever-changing processes.

---

<a name="term-32"></a>
## 32. Pañcakkhandha

**Skt.: pañcaskandha**

**ET:** pañca (five) + *khandha* (aggregate) = "five aggregates."

**DEF:** The five aggregates that constitute what is conventionally called a "being" or "personality." In SN 22.48, they are enumerated as material form (rūpa), feeling (vedanā), perception (saññā), formations (saṅkhārā), and consciousness (viññāṇa). These five are not a permanent self; they are merely natural, conditioned processes. The teaching on the five aggregates is central to the Buddha's analysis of experience and suffering.

**CTX:**
1. **The Anattalakkhana Sutta** — in SN 22.59, the Buddha examines each of the five aggregates and shows that none of them can be taken as "self." He systematically applies three characteristics to each: impermanence (anicca), suffering (dukkha), and non-self (anattā).
2. **As the burden** — in SN 22.22 (Bhāra Sutta), the five aggregates are described as the "burden," craving (taṇhā) as the "taking up of the burden," and nibbāna as the "laying down of the burden."

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the burden? 'The five aggregates subject to clinging,' would be the answer. Which five? Form... feeling... perception... formations... consciousness." (SN 22.22)

**REL:**
- → Khandha (aggregates)
- → Rūpa, Vedanā, Saññā, Saṅkhāra, Viññāṇa (five)
- → Upādānakkhandha (aggregates of clinging)
- → Anattā (non-self)
- → Nāma-rūpa (name-and-form)

**STG:** Direct insight into the five aggregates occurs at the stage of vipassanā. Their true nature is fully understood at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** The five aggregates are the primary object of vipassanā practice. Observing them as they arise and pass away in the present moment leads to direct realization of anicca, dukkha, and anattā.

---

<a name="term-33"></a>
## 33. Rūpakkhandha

**Skt.: rūpaskandha**

**ET:** rūpa (form, matter) + *khandha* (aggregate) = "aggregate of form."

**DEF:** The aggregate of form — includes all material phenomena. In SN 22.48, it is defined as consisting of the four great elements (catudhātu) — earth (paṭhavī), water (āpo), fire (tejo), air (vāyo) — and the forms derived from them (food, body, senses, etc.). This aggregate corresponds to physical experience, including the body and external objects. It is the first of the five aggregates undergoing change and affected by contact.

**CTX:**
1. **In the analysis of aggregates** — in SN 22.48, rūpakkhandha is defined as the material aspect of experience, encompassing both internal and external forms.
2. **As an object of meditation** — in MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta), observing the body in various ways (breathing, postures, parts of the body, elements) is kāyānupassanā, which includes rūpakkhandha.
3. **Impermanence** — in SN 22.59, rūpa is shown to be impermanent: "Form is impermanent... what is impermanent is suffering... what is suffering is not-self."

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the aggregate of form? The four great elements and the form derived from the four great elements." (SN 22.48)

**REL:**
- → Khandha (aggregate)
- → Vedanākkhandha (feeling aggregate)
- → Rūpa (form)
- → Dhātu (element)
- → Kāya (body)

**STG:** Observing rūpakkhandha begins from the earliest stages of satipaṭṭhāna practice and deepens through all stages of vipassanā.

**PRC:** The body is the most accessible object for observing impermanence. Contemplation of rūpakkhandha helps to reduce attachment to the body and understand its impersonal nature.

---

<a name="term-34"></a>
## 34. Vedanākkhandha

**Skt.: vedanāskandha**

**ET:** vedanā (feeling) + *khandha* (aggregate) = "aggregate of feeling."

**DEF:** The aggregate of feeling — the totality of affective sensations experienced through the mind and body. In SN 22.48, it includes three types: pleasant (sukha), unpleasant (dukkha), and neutral (adukkham-asukhā). In MN 10 and MN 148, six classes are distinguished: feeling born of contact through eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. Vedanā is not emotion (which belongs to saṅkhārakkhandha), but the bare "hedonic tone" of experience.

**CTX:**
1. **As a separate aggregate** — in SN 22.48, vedanā is distinguished from saññā (perception) and viññāṇa (consciousness). All three arise together but have different functions: vedanā "feels," saññā "recognizes," viññāṇa "cognizes."
2. **Key link in dependent origination** — vedanā is the seventh link, conditioned by contact (phassa) and leading to craving (taṇhā) (SN 12.2). This is a critical point where the chain can be broken.
3. **Observing vedanā** — vedanānupassanā (contemplation of feelings) is the second foundation of mindfulness in MN 10, essential for breaking the conditioned chain leading to suffering.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the aggregate of feeling? There are six classes of feeling: feeling born of eye-contact... ear-contact... nose-contact... tongue-contact... body-contact... mind-contact." (SN 22.48)

**REL:**
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Khandha (aggregate)
- → Vedanānupassanā (contemplation of feelings)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Phassa (contact)

**STG:** Understanding vedanā is essential at all stages. For the stream-enterer, the understanding of vedanā's nature deepens, and at arahatta it is fully penetrated.

**PRC:** Vedanā is the gate through which craving arises. Practitioners learn to observe vedanā without automatic reactions of liking/disliking, thus breaking the chain of dependent origination at its most accessible point.

---

<a name="term-35"></a>
## 35. Saññākkhandha

**Skt.: saṃjñāskandha**

**ET:** saññā (perception) + *khandha* (aggregate) = "aggregate of perception."

**DEF:** The aggregate of perception — the mental function that recognizes, labels, and conceptualizes objects. In MN 44 (Cūḷavedalla Sutta), it is defined by its function: "It perceives (sañjānāti): it perceives blue, yellow, red, white." It "marks" or "notices" features of an object and creates associations. Saññā is conditioned and impermanent.

**CTX:**
1. **Function of recognition** — in MN 44, saññā is the function that recognizes and assigns labels: blue, yellow, red, white, etc. This is how we conceptually grasp the world.
2. **Six types** — in SN 22.56, six types of saññā are listed: perception of forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tactile objects, and mental objects (dhammā).
3. **Distinction from viññāṇa and vedanā** — in MN 43: all three arise together, but each has a distinct function. Viññāṇa cognizes the object in general, vedanā feels its tone, saññā recognizes its specific features.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the aggregate of perception? There are six classes of perception: perception of forms, perception of sounds, perception of smells, perception of tastes, perception of tactile objects, perception of mental objects." (SN 22.56)

**REL:**
- → Saññā (perception)
- → Khandha (aggregate)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Saṅkhārakkhandha (formation aggregate)
- → Nimitta (sign)

**STG:** Understanding saññā as conditioned and impermanent is part of the development of paññā at all stages.

**PRC:** Saññā creates conceptual labels for experience. Realizing that perception is not objective truth but merely a function of the mind helps to break attachment to concepts and views.

---

<a name="term-36"></a>
## 36. Saṅkhārakkhandha

**Skt.: saṃskāraskandha**

**ET:** saṅkhāra (formation, volitional construction) + *khandha* (aggregate) = "aggregate of formations."

**DEF:** The aggregate of formations — includes all volitional and mental factors not covered by the other four aggregates. In SN 22.56, it is defined as the six classes of volition (cetanā) related to the six sense objects. This aggregate encompasses cetanā (volition), sañcetanā (co-intention), vitakka (applied thought), vicāra (sustained thought), and all other mental factors (cetasikas) except feeling and perception. It is the most complex aggregate, including 50 mental factors.

**CTX:**
1. **As the "kamma-forming" aggregate** — all volitional activity (kamma) arises from this aggregate. In AN 6.63, it is said that "intention (cetanā) is kamma."
2. **In dependent origination** — saṅkhārā as the second link (in the form of abhisaṅkhāra) are conditioned by ignorance (avijjā) and condition consciousness (viññāṇa).
3. **Broad and narrow meanings** — saṅkhārā can refer to (a) the aggregate of formations, (b) the second link of dependent origination (volitional formations), (c) all conditioned things (saṅkhata dhamma), or (d) the body (kāya-saṅkhāra) in breathing.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the aggregate of formations? There are six classes of volition: volition regarding forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tactile objects, mental objects." (SN 22.56)

**REL:**
- → Saṅkhāra (formation)
- → Cetanā (intention)
- → Khandha (aggregate)
- → Abhisaṅkhāra (volitional formation)
- → Kamma (kamma)
- → Cetasika (mental factor)

**STG:** Understanding saṅkhārakkhandha deepens through all stages. The cessation of unwholesome formations and the development of wholesome ones is a central task of practice.

**PRC:** The formations aggregate is where most of the practical work occurs — observing intentions, thoughts, and mental factors as they arise, and learning to direct them in wholesome ways.

---

<a name="term-37"></a>
## 37. Viññāṇakkhandha

**Skt.: vijñānaskandha**

**ET:** viññāṇa (consciousness) + *khandha* (aggregate) = "aggregate of consciousness."

**DEF:** The aggregate of consciousness — the bare knowing or cognizing of an object. In SN 22.56, it is defined as six classes of consciousness: eye-consciousness (cakkhu-viññāṇa), ear-consciousness (sota-viññāṇa), nose-consciousness (ghāna-viññāṇa), tongue-consciousness (jivhā-viññāṇa), body-consciousness (kāya-viññāṇa), and mind-consciousness (mano-viññāṇa). Each arises when a sense faculty contacts its corresponding object. Viññāṇa is not a permanent "soul" but a series of momentary cognitions arising due to conditions.

**CTX:**
1. **Six classes of consciousness** — in SN 22.56, viññāṇa is analyzed into six classes corresponding to the six sense doors. Each class of consciousness arises only when the respective sense faculty, object, and attention meet.
2. **Dependent on nāma-rūpa** — in SN 12.2, viññāṇa is conditioned by saṅkhārā and conditions nāma-rūpa. In SN 12.65, viññāṇa and nāma-rūpa are described as mutually dependent, like two sheaves of reeds leaning on each other.
3. **Not a permanent self** — in SN 22.59 (Anattalakkhana Sutta), viññāṇa is shown to be impermanent, suffering, and not-self.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the aggregate of consciousness? There are six classes of consciousness: eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, mind-consciousness." (SN 22.56)

**REL:**
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Khandha (aggregate)
- → Nāma-rūpa (name-and-form)
- → Saḷāyatana (six sense bases)
- → Citta (mind)
- → Mano (mind as faculty)

**STG:** Direct insight into the conditioned nature of viññāṇa occurs at the stage of vipassanā. Complete understanding is attained at arahatta.

**PRC:** Observing viññāṇa as it arises at the sense doors — seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, touching, thinking — reveals it to be a momentary, conditioned process, not a permanent "knower" or self.

---

<a name="term-38"></a>
## 38. Pañcupādānakkhandha

**Skt.: pañcopādānaskandha**

**ET:** pañca (five) + *upādāna* (clinging) + *khandha* (aggregate) = "five aggregates of clinging."

**DEF:** The five aggregates when they are objects of clinging (upādāna). In SN 22.48, the Buddha distinguishes between the mere aggregates (khandha) and the aggregates of clinging (upādānakkhandha). The difference lies in the presence or absence of attachment and identification. In the unawakened person, all five aggregates become objects of clinging — they are grasped as "mine," "I," or "my self." The first noble truth (dukkha) is defined in brief as "the five aggregates of clinging."

**CTX:**
1. **As the first noble truth** — in SN 56.11, the Buddha says: "In brief, the five aggregates of clinging are suffering." Thus, the very nature of experience when grasped with clinging is dukkha.
2. **Distinction from mere aggregates** — in SN 22.48, the Buddha explains that the five aggregates of clinging are the same five aggregates, but as objects of clinging (upādāna). For the arahant, aggregates remain but without clinging — they are simply "aggregates," not "aggregates of clinging."
3. **All arahants have aggregates** — even arahants experience the five aggregates, but they no longer cling to them (MN 1). Thus, they experience "aggregates" without the suffix "of clinging."

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, are the five aggregates of clinging? The aggregate of form that is subject to clinging, the aggregate of feeling... perception... formations... consciousness that is subject to clinging." (SN 22.48)

**REL:**
- → Upādāna (clinging)
- → Khandha (aggregate)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Pañcakkhandha (five aggregates)
- → Arahant (arahant as one free from clinging)

**STG:** The reduction of clinging to the five aggregates begins at sotāpatti, when sakkāya-diṭṭhi is destroyed. Complete non-clinging is attained at arahatta.

**PRC:** The difference between mere aggregates and aggregates of clinging is experiential: when the mind ceases to grasp at any of the five aggregates, suffering ceases. Meditation reveals how we turn each aggregate into "I" or "mine" and teaches us to let go.

---

<a name="term-39"></a>
## 39. Nāma-rūpa

**Skt.: nāma-rūpa**

**ET:** nāma (name, mental) + *rūpa* (form, material) = "name-and-form."

**DEF:** Name-and-form — the fourth link of dependent origination (SN 12.2). Nāma (the mental aspect) includes feeling (vedanā), perception (saññā), intention (cetanā), contact (phassa), and attention (manasikāra). Rūpa (the material aspect) includes the four great elements and form derived from them. Together, nāma-rūpa represents the psychophysical complex that, together with consciousness (viññāṇa), constitutes what is conventionally called a "being." It is conditioned by consciousness and, in turn, conditions the six sense bases (saḷāyatana).

**CTX:**
1. **As a link of dependent origination** — in SN 12.2: "With consciousness as condition, name-and-form; with name-and-form as condition, the six sense bases."
2. **Mutual dependence with viññāṇa** — in SN 12.65 (Nagara Sutta), the Buddha emphasizes that viññāṇa and nāma-rūpa are mutually dependent: like two sheaves of reeds supporting each other. One cannot exist without the other.
3. **Composition of nāma** — in MN 9 (Sammādiṭṭhi Sutta): "What is name (nāma)? Feeling (vedanā), perception (saññā), intention (cetanā), contact (phassa), attention (manasikāra) — this is called name."

**QUO:**
> "With consciousness as condition, name-and-form; with name-and-form as condition, the six sense bases... Just as two sheaves of reeds stand leaning against each other, so too, with name-and-form as condition, consciousness; with consciousness as condition, name-and-form." (SN 12.65)

**REL:**
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Saḷāyatana (six sense bases)
- → Rūpa (form)
- → Nāma (name, mental)

**STG:** Understanding nāma-rūpa as a conditioned, impersonal process destroys the conception of an independent "self." This knowledge develops through all stages of the path.

**PRC:** Contemplation of nāma-rūpa in meditation is the observation of how each moment of experience consists of mental (nāma) and material (rūpa) components. There is no "one who experiences" — only the process.

---

<a name="term-40"></a>
## 40. Magga

**Skt.: mārga**

**ET:** from *√mṛg* (to search, to track) = "path," "way."

**DEF:** Path — the path leading to the cessation of suffering. In SN 45.8, it is defined as the Noble Eightfold Path (ariya-aṭṭhaṅgika-magga). It also refers to the supramundane path moments (magga-citta) that eradicate defilements. The path is to be developed and cultivated. It is the fourth noble truth.

**CTX:**
1. **As the fourth noble truth** — in SN 56.11, the Buddha proclaims: "This is the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering."
2. **Two aspects: mundane and supramundane** — in MN 117 (Mahācattārīsaka Sutta), the path is described as having two aspects: mundane (lokiya) — factors with taints, leading to favorable rebirth, and supramundane (lokuttara) — the Noble Eightfold Path that leads to liberation.
3. **Path moments in Abhidhamma** — in the Abhidhamma, "magga" also refers to specific moments of supramundane consciousness (sotāpatti-magga, etc.) that cut off defilements.

**QUO:**

## 55. Kammaṭṭhāna

**Skt.: karmasthāna**

**ET:** kamma (action, work) + *ṭhāna* (place, basis) = "place of work," "meditation subject."

**DEF:** A meditation subject or object. In MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta), includes various aspects of observation: body, feelings, mind, dhammas. More broadly, any object used for developing samatha or vipassanā. In the Visuddhimagga, 40 traditional kammaṭṭhāna are listed: 10 kasiṇas, 10 asubha, 10 anussati, 4 brahmavihāra, 4 arūpa, 1 perception of repulsiveness of food, 1 analysis of the four elements.

**QUO:**
> "Here, a bhikkhu abides contemplating the body as a body... feelings as feelings... mind as mind... dhammas as dhammas — ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful, having removed covetousness and displeasure towards the world." (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Satipaṭṭhāna (foundations of mindfulness)
- → Samatha (calm)
- → Vipassanā (insight)
- → Ānāpānasati (mindfulness of breathing)
- → Mettā-bhāvanā (development of loving-kindness)

<a name="term-56"></a>
## 56. Ānāpānasati

**Skt.: ānāpānasmṛti**

**ET:** ānāpāna (in-breath and out-breath) + *sati* (mindfulness) = "mindfulness of breathing."

**DEF:** Mindfulness of breathing — one of the key meditative practices. In MN 118 (Ānāpānasati Sutta), the Buddha describes sixteen steps divided into four tetrads: (1) observing the body (long/short breath, whole body), (2) observing feelings (rapture, happiness, mental formations), (3) observing the mind (mind, gladdening the mind, concentrating the mind, liberating the mind), (4) observing dhammas (impermanence, dispassion, cessation, relinquishment).

**QUO:**
> "Mindfulness of breathing, bhikkhus, when developed and cultivated, brings great fruit and great benefit. Mindfulness of breathing, when developed and cultivated, perfects the four foundations of mindfulness." (MN 118)

**REL:**
- → Satipaṭṭhāna (foundations of mindfulness)
- → Sati (mindfulness)
- → Samādhi (concentration)
- → Jhāna (jhāna)
- → Kāyānupassanā (contemplation of body)

<a name="term-57"></a>
## 57. Satipaṭṭhāna

**Skt.: smṛtyupasthāna**

**ET:** sati (mindfulness) + *paṭṭhāna* (establishment, foundation) = "foundations of mindfulness."

**DEF:** The four foundations of mindfulness — the central practice of Buddhist meditation described in MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta) and DN 22 (Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta). The four foundations: (1) kāyānupassanā — contemplation of the body, (2) vedanānupassanā — contemplation of feelings, (3) cittānupassanā — contemplation of mind, (4) dhammānupassanā — contemplation of dhammas. The Buddha calls this the "sole way" (ekāyano maggo) for the purification of beings and the realization of nibbāna.

**QUO:**
> "This is the sole way, bhikkhus, for the purification of beings, for the overcoming of sorrow and lamentation, for the disappearance of pain and displeasure, for the attainment of the true way, for the realization of nibbāna — namely, the four foundations of mindfulness." (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Sammāsati (right mindfulness)
- → Kāyānupassanā, Vedanānupassanā, Cittānupassanā, Dhammānupassanā
- → Ekāyano maggo (the sole way)

<a name="term-58"></a>
## 58. Mettā-bhāvanā

**Skt.: maitrī-bhāvanā**

**ET:** mettā (loving-kindness) + *bhāvanā* (development) = "development of loving-kindness."

**DEF:** The practice of developing boundless loving-kindness. In SN 46.54 (Mettā Sutta), the Buddha describes mettā as the antidote to hatred (dosa) and ill-will (vyāpāda). The practice involves directing thoughts of loving-kindness first towards oneself, then towards close ones, neutral people, difficult people, and finally towards all beings without exception. Mettā is the first of the four brahmavihāras (divine abodes).

**QUO:**
> "Just as a mother protects her only child even at the risk of her own life, so one should develop boundless loving-kindness towards all beings." (SN 1.8)

**REL:**
- → Brahmavihāra (divine abodes)
- → Karuṇā (compassion)
- → Muditā (sympathetic joy)
- → Upekkhā (equanimity)
- → Adosa (non-hatred)

<a name="term-59"></a>
## 59. Asubha-bhāvanā

**Skt.: aśubha-bhāvanā**

**ET:** asubha (unattractive, repulsive) + *bhāvanā* (development) = "development of the perception of unattractiveness."

**DEF:** The practice of contemplating the unattractiveness of the body. In MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta), this includes contemplating corpses at various stages of decomposition: bloated, bluish, festering, cut up, gnawed, scattered, bloody, worm-infested, skeleton. The aim is to overcome attachment to the body and sensual pleasures. It is the direct antidote to sensual passion (kāma-rāga).

**QUO:**
> "Further, bhikkhus, as if he saw a corpse thrown in a charnel ground, decomposed, blue-black... and he compares this his own body with it: 'This body is of the same nature, it will become like that, it is not exempt from that fate.'" (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Kāyānupassanā (contemplation of body)
- → Kāma-rāga (sensual passion)
- → Subha (attractive)
- → Saññā (perception)
- → Nekkhamma (renunciation)

<a name="term-60"></a>
## 60. Maraṇānussati

**Skt.: maraṇānusmṛti**

**ET:** maraṇa (death) + *anussati* (recollection, mindfulness) = "mindfulness of death."

**DEF:** The practice of contemplating the inevitability of death. In AN 6.19 (Maraṇassati Sutta), the Buddha says that mindfulness of death, when developed and cultivated, brings great fruit and great benefit, leading to the deathless (nibbāna). The practitioner reflects: "Death is certain. Life is uncertain. I will die. My body will be abandoned." This is not morbid reflection but a realistic reminder that inspires diligence in practice.

**QUO:**
> "Mindfulness of death, bhikkhus, when developed and cultivated, brings great fruit and great benefit, it culminates in the deathless, it culminates in the deathless." (AN 6.19)

**REL:**
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Anussati (recollection)
- → Appamāda (diligence)
- → Viriya (energy)
- → Saṃvega (spiritual urgency)

<a name="term-61"></a>
## 61. Brahmacariya

**Skt.: brahmacarya**

**ET:** brahma (highest, holy) + *cariya* (conduct, life) = "holy life."

**DEF:** The holy life leading to liberation. In DN 2 (Sāmaññaphala Sutta), it is described as the path of practice under the guidance of the Buddha. Includes morality (sīla), concentration (samādhi), and wisdom (paññā). It is a complete way of life, not a separate practice. Its goal is the cessation of suffering, culminating in the attainment of nibbāna.

**QUO:**
> "Here, great king, a monk is accomplished in morality, guards the sense doors, possesses mindfulness and clear comprehension, is content with little." (DN 2)

**REL:**
- → Sīla (morality)
- → Samādhi (concentration)
- → Paññā (wisdom)
- → Pabbajjā (going forth)
- → Gṛhastha (householder)

<a name="term-62"></a>
## 62. Viriya (Energy)

**Skt.: vīrya**

**ET:** from *√vīr* (to be strong, heroic) = "energy," "diligence," "effort."

**DEF:** Energy, diligence, effort in practice. In SN 45.8, it is part of sammāvāyāma (right effort). In SN 2.10 (Appamāda Sutta), the Buddha says: "Diligence (appamāda) is the path to the deathless. Heedlessness (pamāda) is the path to death." Viriya is not merely physical energy but mental resolve and persistence in developing wholesome qualities and eliminating unwholesome ones.

**QUO:**
> "Diligence, bhikkhus, I proclaim. One who is diligent overcomes suffering. Without diligence, it is impossible to overcome suffering." (SN 2.10)

**REL:**
- → Sammāvāyāma (right effort)
- → Appamāda (diligence)
- → Pamāda (heedlessness)
- → Bojjhaṅga (factors of awakening)
- → Thīna-middha (sloth-torpor)

<a name="term-63"></a>
## 63. Sati (Mindfulness)

**Skt.: smṛti**

**ET:** from *√smṛ* (to remember) = "memory," "mindfulness," "attention."

**DEF:** Mindfulness, attentiveness, the ability to remember and sustain attention on an object. In SN 45.8, it is the basis of sammāsati (right mindfulness). In MN 10, it is defined through the four satipaṭṭhānas. Sati is not merely passive attention but active recollection that prevents forgetfulness of the object and the arising of unwholesome states. One of the five spiritual faculties (indriya) and the first of the seven factors of awakening (bojjhaṅga).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the faculty of mindfulness? Here, a bhikkhu, being mindful, possesses supreme mindfulness and recollection. He remembers and recollects what was done and said long ago." (SN 48.10)

**REL:**
- → Sammāsati (right mindfulness)
- → Satipaṭṭhāna (foundations of mindfulness)
- → Sampajañña (clear comprehension)
- → Bojjhaṅga (factors of awakening)
- → Indriya (faculties)

<a name="term-64"></a>
## 64. Paññā (Wisdom)

**Skt.: prajñā**

**ET:** pra- (forward, forth) + *√jñā* (to know) = "discernment," "wisdom."

**DEF:** Wisdom, penetrative understanding of reality. In SN 45.8, it is part of the paññā group (together with sammādiṭṭhi and sammāsaṅkappa). Three kinds of paññā are distinguished: suta-mayā paññā (wisdom born of listening), cintā-mayā paññā (wisdom born of reflection), bhāvanā-mayā paññā (wisdom born of development/meditation).

**QUO:**
> "Develop concentration, bhikkhus. One who is concentrated sees things as they really are." (SN 22.5)

**REL:**
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)

<a name="term-65"></a>
## 65. Yoniso manasikāra

**Skt.: yoniśo manaskāra**

**ET:** yoniso (wisely, from the root) + *manasikāra* (attention, directing the mind) = "wise attention."

**DEF:** Wise attention — the ability to direct the mind to an object in such a way as to see its causes, conditions, and consequences. In AN 1.11–15, the Buddha says that yoniso manasikāra leads to the arising of unarisen wholesome states and the strengthening of arisen ones. Its opposite is ayoniso manasikāra (unwise attention), which leads to the arising of unwholesome states.

**QUO:**
> "I do not see any other thing, bhikkhus, that so greatly contributes to the arising of unarisen wholesome states and the strengthening of arisen wholesome states as wise attention." (AN 1.11)

**REL:**
- → Ayoniso manasikāra (unwise attention)
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)
- → Paññā (wisdom)
- → Manasikāra (attention)
- → Āsava (taints)

<a name="term-66"></a>
## 66. Ayoniso manasikāra

**Skt.: ayoniśo manaskāra**

**ET:** a- (not) + *yoniso manasikāra* (wise attention) = "unwise attention."

**DEF:** Unwise attention — directing the mind to an object without understanding its nature, consequences, causes, and conditions. In AN 1.11–15, the Buddha says that ayoniso manasikāra leads to the arising of unarisen unwholesome states and the strengthening of arisen ones. It is attention that grasps at signs (nimitta) and details (anuvyañjana) of objects without seeing their danger (ādīnava).

**QUO:**
> "I do not see any other thing, bhikkhus, that so greatly contributes to the arising of unarisen unwholesome states and the strengthening of arisen unwholesome states as unwise attention." (AN 1.11)

**REL:**
- → Yoniso manasikāra (wise attention)
- → Avijjā (ignorance)
- → Abhijjhā (covetousness)

<a name="term-67"></a>
## 67. Idappaccayatā

**Skt.: idaṃpratyayatā**

**ET:** idaṃ (this) + *paccaya* (condition) + *tā* (abstract noun suffix) = "conditionality," "specific conditionality."

**DEF:** Conditionality — the principle "when this exists, that comes to be." This is the essence of dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda). In SN 12.20 (Paccaya Sutta), the Buddha says: "When this exists, that comes to be. With the arising of this, that arises. When this does not exist, that does not come to be. With the cessation of this, that ceases."

**QUO:**
> "When this exists, that comes to be. With the arising of this, that arises. When this does not exist, that does not come to be. With the cessation of this, that ceases." (SN 12.20)

**REL:**

- → Paccaya (condition)

<a name="term-68"></a>
## 68. Pariyatti / Paṭipatti / Paṭivedha

**Skt.: pariyatti / pratipatti / prativedha**

**ET:** pariyatti from *pari-* (around) + *√i* (to go) = "study," "theory"; paṭipatti from *paṭi-* (back, towards) + *√pad* (to go) = "practice," "application"; paṭivedha from *paṭi-* + *√vidh* (to penetrate) = "penetration," "realization."

**DEF:** Three aspects of the Dhamma: (1) pariyatti — study of the teaching, theoretical knowledge; (2) paṭipatti — practical application of the teaching in meditation and daily life; (3) paṭivedha — direct penetration, realization of the truth. These three are inseparably linked: without study, practice may be misguided; without practice, study is useless; without realization, neither leads to liberation.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these three trainings. What three? Training in higher morality, training in higher mind, training in higher wisdom." (AN 3.20)

**REL:**
- → Sutta (discourse)
- → Vinaya (discipline)
- → Abhidhamma (higher Dhamma)

- → Vipassanā (insight)

## 69. Kalyāṇa-mitta

**Skt.: kalyāṇa-mitra**

**ET:** kalyāṇa (good, wholesome) + *mitta* (friend) = "good friend."

**DEF:** A good friend — one who supports practice of the Dhamma. In SN 45.2 (Mitta Sutta), the Buddha says that having a good friend is the whole of the holy life (brahmacariya). A good friend is not merely a pleasant person but one who knows the path and can point out mistakes, give advice, and inspire. The Buddha himself is the supreme good friend (satthā devamanussānaṃ).

**QUO:**
> "Having a good friend, a noble friend, a noble companion — this is the whole of the holy life." (SN 45.2)

**REL:**
- → Satthā (teacher)
- → Saṅgha (community)
- → Brahmacariya (holy life)
- → Ovāda (instruction)

<a name="term-70"></a>
## 70. Nīvaraṇa

**Skt.: nīvaraṇa**

**ET:** ni- (down) + *√var* (to obstruct, cover) = "hindrance," "obstruction."

**DEF:** Five hindrances that obstruct concentration and block insight: kāmacchanda (sensual desire), vyāpāda (ill-will), thīna-middha (sloth and torpor), uddhacca-kukkucca (restlessness and remorse), vicikicchā (doubt). In DN 2, they are described as obstacles to jhāna. The Buddha compares a mind overcome by hindrances to water that is stained, turbid, covered with algae — in which one cannot see one's own reflection.

**QUO:**
> "As long as these five hindrances are not abandoned, the bhikkhu considers himself as if in debt, sick, imprisoned, enslaved, on a desert road." (DN 2)

**REL:**
- → Kāmacchanda, Vyāpāda, Thīna-middha, Uddhacca-kukkucca, Vicikicchā
- → Bojjhaṅga (factors of awakening)
- → Jhāna (jhāna)
- → Ayoniso manasikāra (wrong attention)
- → Samādhi (concentration)

<a name="term-71"></a>
## 71. Kāmacchanda

**Skt.: kāmacchanda**

**ET:** kāma (sensual pleasure) + *chanda* (desire, aspiration) = "sensual desire."

**DEF:** Sensual desire — the first of the five hindrances. Arises from ayoniso manasikāra (unwise attention) towards attractive objects. Captures the mind and prevents concentration. In SN 46.55, it is defined as desire directed towards the five sense objects: forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tactile sensations. It is overcome through contemplation of unattractiveness (asubha-bhāvanā) and development of renunciation (nekkhamma).

**QUO:**
> "When sensual desire overcomes a bhikkhu's mind and remains, his mind is called 'not flexible,' 'not wieldy,' 'not capable of concentration' for the destruction of the taints." (AN 5.51)

**REL:**
- → Nīvaraṇa (hindrances)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Asubha-bhāvanā (contemplation of unattractiveness)
- → Nekkhamma (renunciation)
- → Kāma (sensuality)

<a name="term-72"></a>
## 72. Vyāpāda

**Skt.: vyāpāda**

**ET:** vi-ā-√pad (to oppose, harm) = "ill-will," "malevolence."

**DEF:** Ill-will or malevolence — the second hindrance. Includes anger, aversion, hostility, and the wish to cause harm. Destroys the mind's peace and creates kamma leading to rebirth in unhappy planes. It is countered by developing mettā (loving-kindness).

**QUO:**
> "When loving-kindness is developed and cultivated, it abandons hatred." (SN 46.54)

**REL:**
- → Nīvaraṇa (hindrances)
- → Mettā (loving-kindness)
- → Dosa (hatred)
- → Abyāpāda (non-ill-will)
- → Sammāsaṅkappa (right intention)

<a name="term-73"></a>
## 73. Thīna-middha

**Skt.: styāna-middha**

**ET:** thīna (sloth) + *middha* (torpor) = "sloth and torpor."

**DEF:** Sloth and torpor — the third hindrance. A state of mental and physical heaviness, dullness, lack of energy. Impairs attention and effort. It differs from ordinary tiredness: it is a mental state that can arise even with sufficient rest. It is overcome by developing energy (viriya), perception of light (āloka-saññā), and changing posture.

**QUO:**
> "When, bhikkhus, the mind is sluggish, that is the right time for developing the awakening factor of energy... investigation of dhammas... rapture... happiness... tranquility... concentration... equanimity." (SN 46.2)

**REL:**
- → Nīvaraṇa (hindrances)
- → Viriya (energy)
- → Kosajja (laziness)
- → Āloka-saññā (perception of light)
- → Passaddhi (tranquility)

<a name="term-74"></a>
## 74. Uddhacca-kukkucca

**Skt.: auddhatya-kaukṛtya**

**ET:** uddhacca (restlessness, from *√dhā* — to shake) + *kukkucca* (remorse, regret) = "restlessness and remorse."

**DEF:** Mental instability (uddhacca) — a state of agitation, restlessness, inability to settle. Kukkucca — remorse over past unwholesome actions or worry about the future. Together they constitute the fourth hindrance. They prevent concentration because the mind constantly jumps from object to object. They are overcome through tranquility (passaddhi) and acceptance of the past.

**QUO:**
> "When the mind is restless, that is the wrong time for developing calming factors of awakening... When the mind is restless, that is the right time for developing energizing factors of awakening." (SN 46.53)

**REL:**
- → Nīvaraṇa (hindrances)
- → Passaddhi (tranquility)
- → Samādhi (concentration)
- → Kukkucca (remorse)

<a name="term-75"></a>
## 75. Vicikicchā

**Skt.: vicikitsā**

**ET:** vi- (separation) + *√cit* (to think) = "divided thought," doubt.

**DEF:** Doubt — the fifth hindrance. Not intellectual skepticism as investigation, but paralyzing uncertainty that blocks progress. In SN 46.2, eight objects of doubt are listed: doubt about the Buddha, the Dhamma, the Saṅgha, the training, the past, the future, both past and future, and dependent origination. It is overcome through study of the Dhamma, reflection, and direct experience.

**QUO:**
> "When, bhikkhus, the mind is overcome by doubt, that is the wrong time for developing calming factors of awakening... When the mind is overcome by doubt, that is the right time for developing the awakening factor of investigation of dhammas." (SN 46.2)

**REL:**
- → Nīvaraṇa (hindrances)
- → Saddhā (confidence)
- → Dhammavicaya (investigation of dhammas)
- → Pariyatti (study)

<a name="term-76"></a>
## 76. Kilesa (Defilements)

**Skt.: kleśa**

**ET:** from *√klis* (to be defiled, to suffer) = "defilement," "impurity," "stain of mind."

**DEF:** Defilements or impurities of the mind that make it impure and hinder liberation. In the Canon (SN 45.165), ten kilesa are enumerated: lobha (greed), dosa (hatred), moha (delusion), māna (conceit), diṭṭhi (wrong view), vicikicchā (doubt), thīna (sloth), uddhacca (restlessness), ahirikā (lack of shame), anottappa (lack of moral dread). Kilesa are closely related to āsava and anusaya.

**QUO:**
> "Luminous, bhikkhus, is this mind. But it is defiled by adventitious defilements." (AN 1.51)

**REL:**

<a name="term-77"></a>
## 77. Saṃyojana

**Skt.: saṃyojana**

**ET:** saṃ- (together) + *√yuj* (to bind) = "fetters," "bonds" that bind beings to saṃsāra.

**DEF:** Fetters — ten factors that bind beings to the cycle of rebirth. In AN 10.13, they are listed: (1) sakkāya-diṭṭhi (personality view), (2) vicikicchā (doubt), (3) sīlabbata-parāmāsa (clinging to rules and rituals), (4) kāma-rāga (sensual passion), (5) paṭigha (irritation), (6) rūpa-rāga (passion for form), (7) arūpa-rāga (passion for formlessness), (8) māna (conceit), (9) uddhacca (restlessness), (10) avijjā (ignorance).

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these ten fetters. What ten? Personality view, doubt, clinging to rules and rituals, sensual passion, irritation, passion for form, passion for formlessness, conceit, restlessness, ignorance." (AN 10.13)

**REL:**

<a name="term-78"></a>
## 78. Sakkāya-diṭṭhi

**Skt.: satkāya-dṛṣṭi**

**ET:** sakkāya (existing personality, from *sat* (existing) + *kāya* (body, group)) + *diṭṭhi* (view) = "personality view."

**DEF:** Personality view — the first of the ten fetters. It is the identification of the five aggregates (pañcakkhandha) with "self" or "mine." In SN 22.1, it is defined as the view: "This is mine, I am this, this is my self." The destruction of sakkāya-diṭṭhi is the mark of sotāpatti (stream-entry).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is personality view? Here, an uninstructed ordinary person... regards form as self, or self as possessing form, or form as in self, or self as in form..." (SN 22.1)

**REL:**

<a name="term-79"></a>
## 79. Sīlabbata-parāmāsa

**Skt.: śīlavrata-parāmarśa**

**ET:** sīla (morality) + *vata* (vow, practice) + *parāmāsa* (clinging, grasping) = "clinging to rules and rituals."

**DEF:** Clinging to rules and rituals — the third of the ten fetters. The belief that purification is achieved through observing certain rules, rituals, or ascetic practices without developing wisdom. In MN 78 (Samaṇamaṇḍikā Sutta), the Buddha criticizes this view, emphasizing that purification occurs through the removal of defilements, not through external practices.

**QUO:**
> "Some ascetics and brahmins, holding to the belief in vows and practices, consider that purification is attained through vows and practices." (MN 78)

**REL:**
- → Saṃyojana (fetters)
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)
- → Sīla (morality)
- → Vata (vow, practice)
- → Micchādiṭṭhi (wrong view)

<a name="term-80"></a>
## 80. Paṭiccasamuppāda

**Skt.: pratītyasamutpāda**

**ET:** paṭicca (in dependence on, relying on) + *samuppāda* (arising, from *saṃ-* + *√pad* — to arise) = "dependent origination."

**DEF:** The law of conditionally arisen phenomena. The central teaching of early Buddhism describing twelve links (nidāna) that sustain the cycle of saṃsāra. The Buddha said: "Who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma; who sees the Dhamma sees dependent origination" (MN 28). It is not a philosophical theory but a description of how suffering arises and how it can be ceased.

**QUO:**
> "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations [arise]; with volitional formations as condition, consciousness [arises]... Such is the origin of this whole mass of suffering." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Avijjā, Saṅkhārā, Viññāṇa, Nāma-rūpa, Saḷāyatana, Phassa, Vedanā, Taṇhā, Upādāna, Bhava, Jāti, Jarā-maraṇa
- → Idappaccayatā (specific conditionality)
- → Dhammatā (the law of Dhamma)
- → Nirodha (cessation)

---

<a name="term-81"></a>
## 81. Taṇhā

**Skt.: tṛṣṇā**

**ET:** from *√tṛṣ* (to thirst, to desire to drink) = "craving," "thirst."

**DEF:** Craving — the second noble truth, the origin of suffering (dukkha-samudaya). In SN 12.2, it is the eighth link of dependent origination. Three kinds: kāma-taṇhā (craving for sensual pleasures), bhava-taṇhā (craving for existence), vibhava-taṇhā (craving for non-existence). The destruction of taṇhā is nibbāna.

**QUO:**
> "And this, bhikkhus, is the noble truth of the origin of suffering: it is craving that leads to renewed existence, accompanied by enjoyment and passion, seeking delight here and there; that is, craving for sensual pleasures, craving for existence, craving for non-existence." (SN 56.11)

**REL:**
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Nibbāna (cessation of craving)
- → Upādāna (clinging)
- → Rāga (passion)
- → Vedanā (feeling)

<a name="term-82"></a>
## 82. Upādāna

**Skt.: upādāna**

**ET:** upa- (toward, close) + *ā-* + *√dā* (to take) = "grasping," "clinging."

**DEF:** Clinging — the ninth link of dependent origination. Four kinds: kāmupādāna (clinging to sensual pleasures), diṭṭhupādāna (clinging to views), sīlabbatupādāna (clinging to rules and rituals), attavādupādāna (clinging to the doctrine of self). Conditioned by craving (taṇhā) and leads to becoming (bhava). It is a more intense form of taṇhā, where desire intensifies into fixation.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is clinging? There are four kinds of clinging: clinging to sensual pleasures, clinging to views, clinging to rules and rituals, clinging to the doctrine of self. This is called clinging." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Bhava (becoming)
- → Pañcupādānakkhandha (five aggregates of clinging)
- → Kāmupādāna, Diṭṭhupādāna, Sīlabbatupādāna, Attavādupādāna

<a name="term-83"></a>
## 83. Phassa

**Skt.: sparśa**

**ET:** from *√spṛś* (to touch, contact) = "contact."

**DEF:** Contact — the sixth link of dependent origination. Arises from the meeting of a sense faculty (indriya), an object (ārammaṇa), and consciousness (viññāṇa). Six kinds of phassa, corresponding to the six sense faculties: eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, mind-contact. Conditioned by the six sense bases (saḷāyatana) and leads to feeling (vedanā).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is contact? There are six kinds of contact: eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, mind-contact. This is called contact." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Saḷāyatana (six sense bases)
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Āyatana (base)
- → Cakkhu-samphassa, etc. (six kinds of contact)

---

<a name="term-84"></a>
## 84. Vedanā (in context of dependent origination)

**Skt.: vedanā**

**ET:** from *√vid* (to feel, to know) = "feeling," "sensation."

**DEF:** Feeling — the seventh link of dependent origination. Three kinds: pleasant (sukhā), unpleasant (dukkhā), neutral (adukkham-asukhā). Conditioned by contact (phassa) and leads to craving (taṇhā). This is a key point in the chain where interruption is possible: if vedanā does not give rise to taṇhā, the chain is broken. Feeling is not emotion (which belongs to saṅkhārakkhandha), but the bare hedonic tone of experience.

**QUO:**
> "With contact as condition, feeling [arises]." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Phassa (contact)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Vedanānupassanā (contemplation of feelings)
- → Sukhā, Dukkhā, Adukkham-asukhā (three kinds)
- → Saḷāyatana (six sense bases)

<a name="term-85"></a>
## 85. Jāti

**Skt.: jāti**

**ET:** from *√jan* (to be born) = "birth."

**DEF:** Birth — the eleventh link of dependent origination. In SN 12.2, it is defined as the arising in a particular order of beings, the manifestation of the aggregates (khandhānaṃ pātubhāvo), the acquisition of the sense bases (āyatanānaṃ paṭilābho). Conditioned by becoming (bhava) and leads to aging-and-death (jarā-maraṇa).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is birth? The birth of various beings in the respective orders of beings, their appearing, descent [into the womb], arising, manifestation of the aggregates, acquisition of the sense bases — this is called birth." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Bhava (becoming)
- → Jarā-maraṇa (aging and death)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Khandha (aggregates)
- → Āyatana (bases)

<a name="term-86"></a>
## 86. Jarā-maraṇa

**Skt.: jarā-maraṇa**

**ET:** jarā (aging) + *maraṇa* (death) = "aging and death."

**DEF:** Aging and death — the twelfth and final link of dependent origination. Jarā: decrepitude, greying, wrinkling, decline of faculties. Maraṇa: the dissolution of the aggregates, the laying down of the body. With birth as condition, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair arise — the whole mass of suffering.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is aging-and-death? The aging of various beings in the respective orders of beings, their decrepitude, broken teeth, grey hair, wrinkled skin, decline of vitality, weakening of faculties — this is called aging. The death of various beings in the respective orders of beings, their passing, dissolution, disappearance, death, completion of time, dissolution of the aggregates, laying down of the body — this is called death." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Jāti (birth)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Maraṇānussati (mindfulness of death)
- → Khandha (aggregates)

<a name="term-87"></a>
## 87. Saḷāyatana

**Skt.: ṣaḍāyatana**

**ET:** saḷ (six) + *āyatana* (sphere, base) = "six sense bases."

**DEF:** The six sense bases — the fifth link of dependent origination. Includes six internal bases (ajjhattikāni āyatanāni): eye (cakkhu), ear (sota), nose (ghāna), tongue (jivhā), body (kāya), mind (mano); and six external bases (bāhirāni āyatanāni): forms (rūpa), sounds (sadda), smells (gandha), tastes (rasa), tactile objects (phoṭṭhabba), mental objects (dhamma). Conditioned by name-and-form (nāma-rūpa) and leads to contact (phassa).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, are the six sense bases? The eye-base, ear-base, nose-base, tongue-base, body-base, mind-base. These are called the six sense bases." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Nāma-rūpa (name-and-form)
- → Phassa (contact)
- → Āyatana (base)
- → Cakkhu, Sota, Ghāna, Jivhā, Kāya, Mano (six internal bases)
- → Rūpa, Sadda, Gandha, Rasa, Phoṭṭhabba, Dhamma (six external bases)

<a name="term-88"></a>
## 88. Nāma-rūpa (in dependent origination)

**Skt.: nāma-rūpa**

**ET:** nāma (name, mental) + *rūpa* (form, material) = "name-and-form."

**DEF:** Name-and-form — the fourth link of dependent origination. Nāma includes feeling (vedanā), perception (saññā), intention (cetanā), contact (phassa), and attention (manasikāra). Rūpa includes the four great elements and form derived from them. Together they represent the psychophysical complex necessary for the arising of consciousness (viññāṇa).

**QUO:**
> "With consciousness as condition, name-and-form; with name-and-form as condition, the six sense bases... Just as two sheaves of reeds stand leaning against each other, so too, with name-and-form as condition, consciousness; with consciousness as condition, name-and-form." (SN 12.65)

**REL:**
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Saḷāyatana (six sense bases)
- → Rūpa (form)
- → Nāma (name, mental)

<a name="term-89"></a>
## 89. Avijjā (in dependent origination)

**Skt.: avidyā**

**ET:** a- (negative particle) + *vijjā* (knowledge) = "non-knowledge," "ignorance."

**DEF:** Ignorance — the first link of dependent origination. In SN 12.2, it is defined as not knowing the four noble truths. This is the fundamental distortion of perception from which all conditioned processes arise. Avijjā sets the entire chain of saṃsāra in motion: "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations [arise]."

**QUO:**
> "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations [arise]." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Saṅkhārā (formations)
- → Vijjā (knowledge)
- → Moha (delusion)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Sammādiṭṭhi (right view)

<a name="term-90"></a>
## 90. Saṅkhārā (in dependent origination)

**Skt.: saṃskāra**

**ET:** saṃ- (together) + *√kar* (to do) = "co-making," "formations."

**DEF:** Volitional formations — the second link of dependent origination. In SN 12.2, three kinds: bodily (kāya-saṅkhāra), verbal (vacī-saṅkhāra), mental (citta-saṅkhāra). Conditioned by ignorance (avijjā) and lead to consciousness (viññāṇa). These are kammically active intentions that shape future experience.

**QUO:**
> "With ignorance as condition, volitional formations [arise]." (SN 12.2)

**REL:**
- → Avijjā (ignorance)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Cetanā (intention)
- → Kamma (kamma)
- → Abhisaṅkhāra (volitional formations)

<a name="term-91"></a>
## 91. Sotāpatti

**Skt.: srotāpatti**

**ET:** sota (stream) + *āpatti* (entry, attainment) = "stream-entry."

**DEF:** The first stage of awakening. In SN 55.5, the sotāpanna is described as one who has overcome three fetters (saṃyojana): sakkāya-diṭṭhi (personality view), vicikicchā (doubt), sīlabbata-parāmāsa (clinging to rules and rituals). Guaranteed to reach full liberation within at most seven lives. A sotāpanna can no longer be reborn in lower realms (hell, animal realm, ghost realm).

**QUO:**
> "When, bhikkhus, a noble disciple understands the cause and origin of things... he is a stream-enterer, not subject to falling into lower realms, heading towards enlightenment." (SN 55.5)

**REL:**
- → Sakadāgāmitā (once-returning)
- → Anāgāmitā (non-returning)
- → Arahatta (arahantship)
- → Saṃyojana (fetters)
- → Sotāpatti-magga (path of stream-entry)
- → Sotāpanna (stream-enterer)

<a name="term-92"></a>
## 92. Sakadāgāmitā

**Skt.: sakṛdāgāmitā**

**ET:** sakad (once) + *āgāmitā* (returning) = "once-returning."

**DEF:** The second stage of awakening. One who will return to the world of sensual desires only once more. In AN 3.88, it is defined as the weakening (tanubhāva) of sensual passion (kāma-rāga) and ill-will (vyāpāda). A sakadāgāmī will be reborn in the sense world once more and then attain final liberation.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is a once-returner? Here, a certain person has weakened sensual passion, weakened ill-will. He is called a once-returner." (AN 3.88)

**REL:**
- → Sotāpatti (stream-entry)
- → Anāgāmitā (non-returning)
- → Arahatta (arahantship)
- → Kāma-rāga (sensual passion)
- → Vyāpāda (ill-will)

<a name="term-93"></a>
## 93. Anāgāmitā

**Skt.: anāgāmitā**

**ET:** an- (not) + *āgāmitā* (returning) = "non-returning."

**DEF:** The third stage of awakening. One who has completely overcome sensual passion (kāma-rāga) and ill-will (vyāpāda) and will not return to the world of sensual desires (kāma-loka). An anāgāmī after death will be reborn in the form world (rūpa-loka) or formless world (arūpa-loka), where they will attain final liberation (nibbāna).

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is a non-returner? Here, a certain person has completely destroyed the five lower fetters. He is called a non-returner." (AN 3.88)

**REL:**
- → Sakadāgāmitā (once-returning)
- → Arahatta (arahantship)
- → Kāma-rāga (sensual passion)
- → Vyāpāda (ill-will)

<a name="term-94"></a>
## 94. Arahatta

**Skt.: arhattva**

**ET:** from *arahant* (worthy one) = "the state of arahant."

**DEF:** The fourth and highest stage of awakening. Complete destruction of all ten fetters (saṃyojana), including the five higher ones: rūpa-rāga (passion for form), arūpa-rāga (passion for formlessness), māna (conceit), uddhacca (restlessness), avijjā (ignorance). An arahant has attained final liberation (nibbāna), destroyed all taints (āsava), and after death will not be reborn. His actions no longer create kamma.

**QUO:**
> "Birth is destroyed, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more coming back to any state of being." (SN 22.59)

**REL:**
- → Anāgāmitā (non-returning)
- → Arahant (arahant)
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna)
- → Āsavānaṃ khaya (destruction of taints)
- → Vimutti (liberation)

<a name="term-95"></a>
## 95. Phala

**Skt.: phala**

**ET:** from *√phal* (to bear fruit) = "fruit," "result."

**DEF:** The fruit of practice — the direct result of spiritual development. In AN 3.88, four fruits (cattāri phalāni) are listed: fruit of stream-entry (sotāpatti-phala), fruit of once-returning (sakadāgāmi-phala), fruit of non-returning (anāgāmi-phala), fruit of arahantship (arahatta-phala). Phala follows magga (path) as a result follows its cause.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these four fruits of the contemplative life. What four? The fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arahantship." (AN 3.88)

**REL:**
- → Magga (path)
- → Sotāpatti-phala, Sakadāgāmi-phala, Anāgāmi-phala, Arahatta-phala

<a name="term-96"></a>
## 96. Magga-phala

**Skt.: mārga-phala**

**ET:** magga (path) + *phala* (fruit) = "path and fruit."

**DEF:** Path and fruit as an inseparable unity. In SN 45.8, magga (the Noble Eightfold Path) leads to phala (liberation). In Abhidhamma psychology, magga (the moment of insight) and phala (the moment of fruit immediately following magga) are distinguished. Magga-phala describes the dynamics of awakening: the path leads to the fruit, the fruit confirms the correctness of the path.

**QUO:**
> "This Noble Eightfold Path should be developed. And how is the Noble Eightfold Path developed? ... When developed, it brings four fruits." (SN 45.8)

**REL:**
- → Magga (path)
- → Phala (fruit)
- → Sotāpatti-magga/phala, etc.
- → Citta (consciousness)
- → Vipassanā (insight)

<a name="term-97"></a>
## 97. Sotāpanna / Sakadāgāmī / Anāgāmī / Arahant

**Skt.: srotāpanna / sakṛdāgāmin / anāgāmin / arhat**

**ET:** sotāpanna from *sota* (stream) + *āpanna* (entered); sakadāgāmī from *sakad* (once) + *āgāmī* (returner); anāgāmī from *an-* (not) + *āgāmī* (returner); arahant from *√arh* (to be worthy).

**DEF:** Four types of noble persons (ariya-puggala) corresponding to the four stages of awakening. In AN 4.87, the Buddha speaks of four pairs, eight persons: those on the path to each stage (magga) and those who have attained the fruit (phala). These eight persons constitute the Noble Saṅgha (ariya-saṅgha).

**QUO:**
> "Four pairs, eight persons — this is the Saṅgha of the Blessed One's disciples, worthy of gifts, worthy of hospitality, worthy of offerings, worthy of reverential salutation, the unsurpassed field of merit for the world." (AN 4.87)

## 98. Āsavānaṃ khaya

**Skt.: āsravakṣaya**

## 99. Sati (as mental faculty)

## 100. Paññā (as mental faculty)

**Skt.: prajñā**

**ET:** pa- (intensifying prefix) + *√ñā* (to know) = "wisdom," "higher knowledge."

**DEF:** Wisdom — one of the five spiritual faculties (indriya). In SN 48.10, paññā-indriya is defined as understanding the four noble truths. Three kinds: suta-mayā paññā (wisdom born of listening), cintā-mayā paññā (wisdom born of reflection), bhāvanā-mayā paññā (wisdom born of development/meditation). ## 101. Saddhā

**Skt.: śraddhā**

**ET:** from *√śrad* (to trust, believe) = "faith," "confidence."

**Skt.: vīrya**

## 103. Samādhi (as spiritual faculty)

**Skt.: samādhi**

**ET:** sam- (together) + *ā-* + *√dhā* (to place, put) = "gathering together," "concentration."

**DEF:** Concentration, collectedness of mind on a single object. In SN 48.10, it is the fourth of the five spiritual faculties (indriya). In its full form, it corresponds to the four jhānas. When developed, samādhi becomes a power (bala) that cannot be shaken by distractions.

**QUO:**

<a name="term-104"></a>
## 104. Indriya

**Skt.: indriya**

**ET:** from *√ind* (to possess power) = "faculty," "spiritual organ."

**DEF:** Five spiritual faculties: saddhā (faith), viriya (energy), sati (mindfulness), samādhi (concentration), paññā (wisdom). In SN 48.10, each is described in detail. When fully developed, they become powers (bala) that cannot be shaken. These five factors must be balanced: too much saddhā without paññā leads to blind faith; too much paññā without saddhā leads to skepticism.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these five faculties. What five? The faculty of faith, the faculty of energy, the faculty of mindfulness, the faculty of concentration, the faculty of wisdom." (SN 48.10)

<a name="term-105"></a>
## 105. Bala

**Skt.: bala**

**ET:** from *√bal* (to be strong) = "power," "strength."

**DEF:** Five powers: the same five factors as indriya, but at the stage where they have become unshakeable. In AN 5.14–15, they are listed: saddhā-bala (power of faith), viriya-bala (power of energy), sati-bala (power of mindfulness), samādhi-bala (power of concentration), paññā-bala (power of wisdom). These powers cannot be shaken by opposing states: doubt, laziness, forgetfulness, distraction, ignorance.

**QUO:**

<a name="term-106"></a>
## 106. Bojjhaṅga

**Skt.: bodhyaṅga**

**ET:** bodhi (awakening) + *aṅga* (factor, part) = "factor of awakening."

**DEF:** Seven factors of awakening: sati (mindfulness), dhammavicaya (investigation of dhammas), viriya (energy), pīti (rapture), passaddhi (tranquility), samādhi (concentration), upekkhā (equanimity). In SN 46, they are described as the path to nibbāna. These factors are interdependent: development of one supports the development of others.

**QUO:**
> "These seven factors of awakening, bhikkhus, when developed and cultivated, lead to direct knowledge, to awakening, to nibbāna." (SN 46.1)

**REL:**
- → Sati, Dhammavicaya, Viriya, Pīti, Passaddhi, Samādhi, Upekkhā

<a name="term-107"></a>
## 107. Dhammavicaya

**Skt.: dharmavicaya**

**ET:** dhamma (phenomena, teachings) + *vicaya* (investigation, analysis) = "investigation of dhammas."

**DEF:** Investigation of dhammas — the second factor of awakening. The ability to analyze phenomena, distinguish wholesome from unwholesome, and understand their nature. In SN 46.2, it is defined as a factor to be developed when the mind is sluggish. It is not intellectual analysis but direct investigation of experience in meditation.

**QUO:**
> "When, bhikkhus, the mind is sluggish, that is the right time for developing the awakening factor of investigation of dhammas." (SN 46.2)

**REL:**
- → Bojjhaṅga (factors of awakening)
- → Paññā (wisdom)
- → Vicikicchā (doubt)
- → Thīna-middha (sloth-torpor)

<a name="term-108"></a>
## 108. Pīti (as factor of awakening)

**Skt.: prīti**

**ET:** from *√prī* (to delight, be pleased) = "rapture," "joy."

**DEF:** Rapture — the fourth factor of awakening. In SN 46.2, it is defined as a factor to be developed when the mind is sluggish. Five kinds of pīti: minor, momentary, showering, uplifting, pervading. As a bojjhaṅga, pīti arises when the mind delights in wholesome qualities. It differs from sukha (happiness): pīti is energizing joy, sukha is calming happiness.

**QUO:**
> "When, bhikkhus, the mind is sluggish, that is the right time for developing the awakening factor of rapture." (SN 46.2)

**REL:**

## 109. Passaddhi

**Skt.: praśrabdhi**

**ET:** from *√śrabh* (to calm down, be ready) = "tranquility," "serenity."

**DEF:** Tranquility of body and mind — the fifth factor of awakening. In SN 46.2, it is defined as a factor to be developed when the mind is restless. Passaddhi arises after pīti and creates the foundation for samādhi. It removes restlessness (uddhacca) and creates conditions for deep concentration.

**QUO:**

**Skt.: upekṣā**

## 121. Vicāra

**Skt.: vicāra**

**Skt.: jihvā**

**DEF:** Tongue as the organ of taste — the fourth of six internal sense bases. In SN 35.23, it functions together with tastes (rasa) and tongue-consciousness (jivhā-viññāṇa). It is conditioned and impermanent. Not "self." Included in the six sense bases. Its observation leads to insight.

**CTX:**
1. **As internal base** — in SN 35.23, jivhā is part of the six bases.
2. **Impermanence** — in SN 35.4: "The tongue, bhikkhus, is impermanent..."
3. **In the context of nutriment** — jivhā plays a role in the perception of taste of food, related to the concept of nutriment (āhāra).

**QUO:**
> "The tongue, bhikkhus, is impermanent. What is impermanent is suffering. What is suffering is not-self." (SN 35.4)

**REL:**
- → Āyatana (base)
- → Rasa (taste)
- → Jivhā-viññāṇa (tongue-consciousness)
- → Jivhā-samphassa (taste contact)
- → Āhāra (nutriment)

**STG:** Observing jivhā as impermanent and impersonal is part of satipaṭṭhāna practice.

**PRC:** Taste is one of the main objects of sensual desire. Seeing it as just a process helps reduce attachment to food and pleasures of taste.

<a name="term-136"></a>
## 136. Kāya

**Skt.: kāya**

**ET:** from *√ci* (to gather, accumulate) = "body," "collection of parts."

**DEF:** Body as the organ of touch — the fifth of six internal sense bases. In SN 35.23, it functions together with tactile objects (phoṭṭhabba) and body-consciousness (kāya-viññāṇa). It is the physical basis for sensations. Subject to decay. Not permanent. Understanding it leads to dispassion.

**CTX:**
1. **As internal base** — in SN 35.23, kāya is part of the six bases.
2. **Impermanence** — in SN 35.5: "The body, bhikkhus, is impermanent..."
3. **As object of meditation** — in MN 10, kāyānupassanā (contemplation of the body) is the first foundation of mindfulness.

**QUO:**
> "The body, bhikkhus, is impermanent. What is impermanent is suffering. What is suffering is not-self." (SN 35.5)

**REL:**
- → Āyatana (base)
- → Phoṭṭhabba (tactile object)
- → Kāya-viññāṇa (body-consciousness)
- → Kāyānupassanā (contemplation of body)
- → Rūpa (form, material)

**STG:** Contemplation of kāya as impermanent and impersonal is a central practice of satipaṭṭhāna.

**PRC:** The body is the most obvious object for observing impermanence. Contemplation of breathing, postures, and bodily processes develops understanding of anicca and reduces attachment to the body.

<a name="term-137"></a>
## 137. Mano (as sense faculty)

**Skt.: manas**

**ET:** from *√man* (to think, understand) = "mind" as the sixth sense faculty.

**DEF:** Mind as the sixth internal sense faculty (āyatana), corresponding to mental objects (dhammā). In the scheme of six bases (saḷāyatana), mano is the internal base (ajjhattikāyatana) along with cakkhu, sota, ghāna, jivhā, kāya. Its objects are dhammā (mental phenomena). Not to be confused with citta (mind as a stream of states) or viññāṇa (consciousness).

**CTX:**
1. **As the sixth base** — in MN 148 (Chachakka Sutta), mano is the internal base, in dependence on which and the corresponding dhammā, mano-viññāṇa (mental consciousness) arises.
2. **In the scheme of dependent origination** — in SN 12.2, saḷāyatana (six bases) includes mano. Contact (phassa) arises from the meeting of mano, dhammā, and mano-viññāṇa.
3. **Distinction of mano, citta and viññāṇa** — in MN 43 (Mahāvedalla Sutta), these three terms are distinguished: mano is the faculty, citta is the state of mind, viññāṇa is consciousness arising from contact of the faculty with the object.

**QUO:**
> "In dependence on the mind (mano) and mental objects (dhammā) arises mental consciousness (mano-viññāṇa). The meeting of these three is contact (phassa)." (MN 148)

**REL:**
- → Citta (mind as state)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Āyatana (base)
- → Dhammā (mental objects)
- → Mano-viññāṇa (mental consciousness)
- → Saḷāyatana (six bases)

**STG:** Understanding mano as a faculty, not a "self," contributes to the destruction of sakkāya-diṭṭhi. Contemplation of the six bases is part of dhammānupassanā (MN 10).

**PRC:** Distinguishing mano, citta, and viññāṇa helps accurately understand one's experience. When a thought or emotion arises, one can observe: there is the faculty (mano), there is the object (dhamma), there is consciousness (mano-viññāṇa). None of them is "I."

<a name="term-138"></a>
## 138. Ārammaṇa

**Skt.: ālambana**

**ET:** ā- (toward) + *√lamb* (to hang, depend) = "object," "that which the mind rests upon."

**DEF:** Object of perception — that towards which the mind is directed. In MN 18 (Madhupiṇḍika Sutta), it plays a key role in the formation of experience. Objects can be external (forms, sounds, etc.) or internal (thoughts, feelings, memories). They trigger mental reactions. Their nature is conditioned. The mind clings to ārammaṇa through saññā (perception) and vedanā (feeling).

**CTX:**
1. **In the perceptual process** — in MN 18: "In dependence on the eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises. The meeting of the three is contact. With contact as condition, feeling. What one feels, that one perceives. What one perceives, that one thinks about. What one thinks about, that one mentally proliferates."
2. **Six types of objects** — corresponding to the six sense faculties: forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tactile objects, mental objects.
3. **As basis for hindrances** — attractive ārammaṇa can arouse kāmacchanda, unattractive ones — vyāpāda.

**QUO:**
> "In dependence on the eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises. The meeting of the three is contact." (MN 18)

**REL:**
- → Āyatana (base)
- → Saññā (perception)
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Phassa (contact)
- → Nimitta (sign)

**STG:** Observing ārammaṇa as mere objects, not as "desirable" or "repulsive," develops at all stages.

**PRC:** Realizing that objects themselves are neutral, and our reactions to them are conditioned, helps reduce automatic responses of attachment and aversion.

<a name="term-139"></a>
## 139. Nimitta

**Skt.: nimitta**

**ET:** from *√mi* (to establish, measure) = "sign," "mark," "image," "impression."

**DEF:** A sign or characteristic of an object of perception. In the context of practice — a mental image or characteristic that the mind grasps when perceiving an object. In MN 10, nimitta is mentioned as that which the mind may follow under wrong attention (ayoniso manasikāra). In the context of meditation — an object of concentration (e.g., uggaha-nimitta — the acquired sign in ānāpānasati practice). The mind grasps nimitta and based on it creates perception (saññā) and concepts.

**CTX:**
1. **As what the mind follows** — in MN 10: "He does not follow the sign (nimitta) of forms..." — meaning the practitioner does not allow the mind to get carried away by the characteristics of objects.
2. **As the basis of wrong attention** — in AN 1.11–15, ayoniso manasikāra (wrong attention) is defined as attention to the nimitta of an attractive object, which leads to the arising of abhijjhā.
3. **In meditation** — in samatha practice, three types are distinguished: parikamma-nimitta (preparatory sign), uggaha-nimitta (acquired sign), and paṭibhāga-nimitta (counterpart sign).

**QUO:**
> "He does not follow the sign (nimitta) of forms, the details (anuvyañjana) of forms." (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Ayoniso manasikāra (wrong attention)
- → Yoniso manasikāra (wise attention)
- → Saññā (perception)
- → Ārammaṇa (object)
- → Anuvyañjana (detailed sign)

**STG:** Working with nimitta is part of satipaṭṭhāna practice at all stages. The ability not to follow signs of objects develops with progress on the path.

**PRC:** Controlling attention to nimitta is the key to preventing the arising of unwholesome states. If the mind does not grasp at attractive signs of an object, abhijjhā does not arise.

## 140. Saññā

**Skt.: saṃjñā**

**ET:** saṃ- (together) + *√jñā* (to know) = "perception," "recognition."

**DEF:** Perception — the ability to distinguish and recognize objects, to assign labels to them. In MN 44 (Cūḷavedalla Sutta), saññā is described as the function that "perceives" (sañjānāti): "It perceives blue, yellow, red, white." Perception shapes conceptual reality by labeling objects. It is impermanent and conditioned. Understanding its nature destroys illusions.

**CTX:**
1. **Function of recognition** — in MN 44: "And what is perception? It perceives: blue, yellow, red, white... This is called perception."
2. **Distinction from vedanā** — in MN 43: vedanā is feeling (pleasant/unpleasant/neutral), while saññā is recognition (blue/yellow/red). Both arise together but have different functions.
3. **Six types of perception** — in SN 22.56: perception of forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tactile objects, and mental objects.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is perception? There are six classes of perception: perception of forms, perception of sounds, perception of smells, perception of tastes, perception of tactile objects, perception of mental objects." (SN 22.56)

**REL:**
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Viññāṇa (consciousness)
- → Saṅkhārā (formations)
- → Nimitta (sign)
- → Saññākkhandha (aggregate of perception)

**STG:** Understanding saññā as conditioned and impermanent is part of developing paññā at all stages.

**PRC:** Saññā creates conceptual labels for experience. Realizing that perception is not objective truth but merely a function of the mind helps break attachment to concepts.

## 141. Sañcetanā

**Skt.: saṃcetanā**

**ET:** saṃ- (together) + *cetanā* (intention) = "co-intention," "collective intention."

**DEF:** Collective intention — a more complex form of cetanā, including planning, pondering, and elaborating actions. In SN 12.38 (Cetanā Sutta), it is said: "What one intends, and what one plans, and whatever one has a tendency towards — this becomes a basis for the establishing of consciousness." Sañcetanā creates more stable kammic patterns than simple momentary intention.

**CTX:**
1. **As condition for consciousness** — in SN 12.38: "What one intends (cetanā), and what one plans (paṇidhī), and whatever one has a tendency towards (anuseti) — this becomes a basis for the establishing of consciousness."
2. **Connection with saṅkhārā** — sañcetanā is part of the saṅkhārakkhandha (aggregate of formations) and creates kammic traces.
3. **Distinction from cetanā** — cetanā is momentary intention; sañcetanā is more developed, including planning and deliberation.

**QUO:**
> "What one intends, and what one plans, and whatever one has a tendency towards — this becomes a basis for the establishing of consciousness." (SN 12.38)

**REL:**
- → Cetanā (intention)
- → Saṅkhārā (formations)
- → Paṇidhī (planning)
- → Anuseti (tendency)
- → Kamma (kamma)

**STG:** Awareness of sañcetanā is important at all stages for understanding how kamma is created.

**PRC:** Observing not only momentary intentions but also deeper plans and tendencies helps see the kammic patterns that shape our lives.

<a name="term-142"></a>
## 142. Saṅkappa

**Skt.: saṅkalpa**

**ET:** saṃ- (together) + *√klṛp* (to form, arrange) = "intention," "resolve," "mental construction."

**DEF:** Intention, resolve, mental construction — close to vitakka but more general. In the context of sammāsaṅkappa (right intention), it is the direction of the mind towards the wholesome: nekkhamma-saṅkappa (intention of renunciation), abyāpāda-saṅkappa (intention of good-will), avihiṃsā-saṅkappa (intention of harmlessness). Saṅkappa shapes the direction of the mind and precedes actions.

**CTX:**
1. **As right intention** — in SN 45.8, sammāsaṅkappa is the second factor of the Noble Eightfold Path.
2. **Unwholesome resolves** — opposites: kāma-saṅkappa (sensual resolve), vyāpāda-saṅkappa (ill-will), vihiṃsā-saṅkappa (resolve to harm).
3. **Connection with vitakka** — saṅkappa is more general; vitakka is a more specific directing of the mind to an object.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is right intention? Intention of renunciation, intention of good-will, intention of harmlessness. This is called right intention." (SN 45.8)

**REL:**
- → Sammāsaṅkappa (right intention)
- → Vitakka (applied thought)
- → Cetanā (intention)
- → Nekkhamma (renunciation)
- → Abyāpāda (good-will)

**STG:** Developing sammāsaṅkappa begins from the very start of the path and is perfected at all stages.

**PRC:** Saṅkappa determines the direction of the mind. If one's resolves are wholesome, actions will be wholesome. Working with saṅkappa is working with motivation at the mind level.

<a name="term-143"></a>
## 143. Chanda

**Skt.: chanda**

**ET:** from *√chand* (to desire, wish) = "desire," "motivation," "will to act."

**DEF:** Desire or motivation — a mental factor that can be either wholesome or unwholesome. In the wholesome sense — chanda as the desire to do good, the desire to practice, the desire to attain liberation (dhammacchanda). In the unwholesome sense — chanda as sensual desire (kāmacchanda). In SN 51.15, chanda is one of the four bases of psychic power (iddhipāda): chanda, viriya, citta, vīmaṃsā.

**CTX:**
1. **As a basis of psychic power** — in SN 51.15: "Bhikkhus, there are these four bases of psychic power. What four? The basis of psychic power consisting of concentration due to desire (chanda) and volitional formations of striving..."
2. **Wholesome and unwholesome** — in AN 6.107, kusala-chanda (wholesome desire) and akusala-chanda (unwholesome desire) are distinguished.
3. **As motivation for practice** — dhammacchanda (desire for the Dhamma) is a healthy motivation, unlike kāmacchanda (sensual desire).

**QUO:**
> "Bhikkhus, there are these four bases of psychic power. What four? The basis of psychic power consisting of concentration due to desire (chanda) and volitional formations of striving..." (SN 51.15)

**REL:**
- → Iddhipāda (bases of psychic power)
- → Kāmacchanda (sensual desire)
- → Dhammacchanda (desire for Dhamma)
- → Viriya (energy)
- → Cetanā (intention)

**STG:** Chanda as wholesome desire develops at all stages. At higher stages, it becomes natural and spontaneous.

**PRC:** Without chanda (desire to practice), there is no motivation. However, it is important to distinguish between the healthy desire to attain liberation and the unhealthy desire for special states or recognition.

<a name="term-144"></a>
## 144. Uppāda

**Skt.: utpāda**

**ET:** ud- (up, out) + *√pad* (to step, go) = "arising," "appearance."

**DEF:** Arising or appearance of phenomena. In SN 22.37 (Pavatta Sutta), it is described as part of the dynamic of conditioned existence: "arising (uppāda), stability (ṭhiti), change (vipariṇāma), disappearance (vaya)." Uppāda is always temporary and dependent on causes and conditions. Understanding this destroys the illusion of stability and leads to the realization of impermanence (anicca).

**CTX:**
1. **As part of the process** — in SN 22.37: "Arising, stability, change, and disappearance of form... feeling... perception... formations... consciousness."
2. **In the context of dependent origination** — uppāda describes the moment when a link of the chain arises in the presence of suitable conditions.
3. **Universality** — all conditioned phenomena (saṅkhata dhamma) are subject to uppāda. Only the unconditioned (asaṅkhata) — nibbāna — does not arise and does not cease.

**QUO:**
> "Arising, stability, change, and disappearance of form... feeling... perception... formations... consciousness — this is called the arising of the aggregates." (SN 22.37)

**REL:**
- → Vaya (disappearance)
- → Vipariṇāma (change)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Saṅkhata (conditioned)
- → Ṭhiti (stability)

**STG:** Observing uppāda in real time is a key aspect of vipassanā. It develops at all stages.

**PRC:** Direct seeing of the arising of phenomena (e.g., the arising of a thought, feeling, bodily sensation) shows their impermanent and impersonal nature. This weakens clinging.

<a name="term-145"></a>
## 145. Vaya

**Skt.: vyaya**

**ET:** from *√vī* (to diminish, disappear) = "disappearance," "decay."

**DEF:** Disappearance or decay of phenomena. In SN 22.37, it is part of the four-phase process along with uppāda, ṭhiti, and vipariṇāma. Whatever has arisen (uppāda) is subject to disappearance (vaya). This is a continuous process showing the instability of all conditioned phenomena. Realizing vaya leads to liberation from attachment.

**CTX:**
1. **As part of the process** — in SN 22.37, vaya completes the cycle of arising, stability, and change.
2. **In the context of impermanence** — vaya is the most obvious manifestation of anicca. What disappears is impermanent.
3. **As an object of contemplation** — in vipassanā practice, observing vaya (the disappearance of feelings, thoughts, bodily sensations) develops understanding of impermanence.

**QUO:**
> "Arising, stability, change, and disappearance of form... feeling... perception... formations... consciousness — this is called the disappearance of the aggregates." (SN 22.37)

**REL:**
- → Uppāda (arising)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Vipariṇāma (change)
- → Nirodha (cessation)
- → Saṅkhata (conditioned)

**STG:** Observing vaya develops at all stages of vipassanā. Complete understanding is at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Seeing how phenomena constantly disappear destroys the illusion of permanence and reduces clinging to experience.

<a name="term-146"></a>
## 146. Vipariṇāma

**Skt.: vipariṇāma**

**ET:** vi- (change) + *pari-* (around) + *√am* (to go) = "change," "transformation."

**DEF:** Change or transformation of phenomena. In SN 22.37, it is the third phase of the process between stability (ṭhiti) and disappearance (vaya). Even seemingly stable states are constantly changing. This is an aspect of impermanence showing the unreliability of experience. Realizing vipariṇāma destroys attachment to apparent stability.

**CTX:**
1. **As part of the process** — in SN 22.37, vipariṇāma follows ṭhiti and precedes vaya.
2. **As a type of suffering** — vipariṇāma-dukkhatā (suffering due to change) is one of the three types of dukkha. Even pleasant states become suffering when they change.
3. **Universality** — all conditioned phenomena are subject to vipariṇāma, from gross material objects to subtle mental states.

**QUO:**
> "Arising, stability, change, and disappearance of form... feeling... perception... formations... consciousness — this is called the change of the aggregates." (SN 22.37)

**REL:**
- → Uppāda (arising)
- → Vaya (disappearance)
- → Ṭhiti (stability)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Dukkha (suffering)

**STG:** Observing vipariṇāma develops understanding of impermanence at all stages.

**PRC:** Understanding that even pleasant states will change prevents clinging to them. This develops equanimity (upekkhā).

<a name="term-147"></a>
## 147. Ṭhiti

**Skt.: sthiti**

**ET:** from *√sthā* (to stand, abide) = "stability," "abiding."

**DEF:** Stability or abiding of phenomena — the second phase in the four-phase process (uppāda, ṭhiti, vipariṇāma, vaya). In SN 22.37, it is described as a temporary stability between arising and change. However, even ṭhiti is not absolute stability — it is merely relative stability before inevitable change. The illusion of permanence (nicca-saññā) arises because the mind does not notice subtle changes during ṭhiti.

**CTX:**
1. **As part of the process** — in SN 22.37, ṭhiti follows uppāda and precedes vipariṇāma.
2. **Relative stability** — even in the phase of ṭhiti, phenomena undergo subtle changes. There is no absolute stability.
3. **As the basis of illusion** — the mind, not seeing subtle changes, creates the illusion of permanence, which leads to attachment.

**QUO:**
> "Arising, stability, change, and disappearance of form... feeling... perception... formations... consciousness — this is called the stability of the aggregates." (SN 22.37)

**REL:**
- → Uppāda (arising)
- → Vipariṇāma (change)
- → Vaya (disappearance)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Nicca-saññā (perception of permanence)

**STG:** Observing even relative stability (ṭhiti) as impermanent develops understanding of anicca.

**PRC:** Seeing that even apparent stability is just a phase in a continuous process of change destroys attachment to "solid" things (body, relationships, possessions).

<a name="term-148"></a>
## 148. Jarā

**Skt.: jarā**

**ET:** from *√jṛ* (to age, wear out) = "aging."

**DEF:** Aging — the process of gradual decay of the body. In SN 12.2, it is part of the link jarā-maraṇa (aging-and-death) completing the chain of dependent origination. It includes decrepitude, greying, wrinkling, decline of strength, weakening of faculties. It is an inevitable aspect of conditioned existence. Realizing aging strengthens the urgency for liberation (saṃvega).

**CTX:**
1. **As part of jarā-maraṇa** — in SN 12.2: "And what, bhikkhus, is aging? The aging of various beings in their respective orders, their decrepitude, broken teeth, grey hair, wrinkled skin, decline of vitality, weakening of faculties — this is called aging."
2. **As an aspect of dukkha** — in SN 56.11, jarā is listed as one of the aspects of the first noble truth: "Aging is suffering."
3. **As an object of contemplation** — in MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta), contemplation of the aging of the body is part of kāyānupassanā.

**QUO:**
> "Aging, bhikkhus, is suffering." (SN 56.11)

**REL:**
- → Maraṇa (death)
- → Jarā-maraṇa (aging and death)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Saṃvega (spiritual urgency)

**STG:** Contemplation of jarā develops a sense of urgency (saṃvega) at all stages, especially at the beginning.

**PRC:** Remembrance of aging (jarānussati) is a powerful practice for developing renunciation and diligence in practice.

<a name="term-149"></a>
## 149. Maraṇa

**Skt.: maraṇa**

**ET:** from *√mṛ* (to die) = "death."

**DEF:** Death — the dissolution of the body and cessation of life functions. In SN 12.2, it completes the chain of dependent origination as part of jarā-maraṇa. It is inevitable for all conditioned beings. Associated with suffering, as it means separation from the beloved body, loved ones, and possessions. Understanding death leads to renunciation (nekkhamma) and diligence in practice.

**CTX:**
1. **As part of jarā-maraṇa** — in SN 12.2: "And what, bhikkhus, is death? The death of various beings in their respective orders, their passing away, dissolution, disappearance, dying, completion of time, dissolution of the aggregates, laying down of the body — this is called death."
2. **As an aspect of dukkha** — in SN 56.11: "Death is suffering."
3. **As an object of recollection** — maraṇānussati (recollection of death) is one of the eight anussati.

**QUO:**
> "Death, bhikkhus, is suffering." (SN 56.11)

**REL:**
- → Jarā (aging)
- → Jarā-maraṇa (aging and death)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Maraṇānussati (recollection of death)
- → Saṃvega (spiritual urgency)

**STG:** Recollection of death is important at all stages, especially at the beginning for developing a sense of urgency.

**PRC:** Maraṇānussati destroys procrastination in practice. When we remember that we can die at any moment, the desire to postpone serious practice to "someday" disappears.

<a name="term-150"></a>
## 150. Nirodha (cessation)

**Skt.: nirodha**

**ET:** ni- (down, cessation) + *√rudh* (to obstruct, stop) = "cessation," "extinction."

**DEF:** Cessation of processes. In SN 12.2, it is the key link of liberation in the reverse order of dependent origination. The third noble truth — cessation of suffering (dukkha-nirodha). This is not the destruction of an essence, but the cessation of the conditions that maintain the process. Cessation of suffering is possible. Understanding nirodha leads to liberation.

**CTX:**
1. **Third noble truth** — in SN 56.11: "This is the noble truth of the cessation of suffering: the complete fading away and cessation of that same craving, its abandonment and relinquishment, freedom from it, non-reliance on it."
2. **In the reverse order of dependent origination** — in SN 12.2: "With the complete cessation of ignorance, formations cease... thus the cessation of this whole mass of suffering."
3. **As a synonym for nibbāna** — nirodha is often used as a synonym for nibbāna, especially in the expression "cessation of lust, hatred, and delusion."

**QUO:**
> "This is the noble truth of the cessation of suffering: the complete fading away and cessation of that same craving, its abandonment and relinquishment, freedom from it, non-reliance on it." (SN 56.11)

**REL:**
- → Dukkha-nirodha (cessation of suffering)
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Vaya (disappearance)

**STG:** Understanding nirodha as the possibility of the cessation of suffering develops at all stages. Direct experience of nirodha occurs at the level of sotāpatti and beyond.

**PRC:** Faith that the cessation of suffering is possible gives hope and direction for practice. This is not a pessimistic teaching, but a realistic one offering a way out.

<a name="term-151"></a>
## 151. Anicca (impermanence)

**Skt.: anitya**

**ET:** na (not) + *nicca* (constant, stable) = "impermanent."

**DEF:** Impermanence of all conditioned phenomena (saṅkhata dhamma). In the context of time and decay — a universal characteristic manifesting through the processes of uppāda, ṭhiti, vipariṇāma, vaya. Everything that arises is subject to decay. The key to insight into the nature of experience. Awareness of anicca destroys the illusion of stability (nicca-saññā) and leads to disenchantment (nibbidā) and letting go.

**CTX:**
1. **Through the five aggregates** — in SN 22.59, the Buddha shows that form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are impermanent.
2. **As a basis for insight** — in MN 64, understanding anicca leads to nibbidā (disenchantment) and virāga (dispassion), which in turn leads to liberation.
3. **Universality** — anicca encompasses all levels of experience, from material objects to subtle mental states. Even jhānas and formless attainments are impermanent.

**QUO:**
> "Form, bhikkhus, is impermanent. What is impermanent is suffering. What is suffering is not-self. What is not-self — this is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self." (SN 22.59)

**REL:**
- → Uppāda, Ṭhiti, Vipariṇāma, Vaya (phases of impermanence)
- → Anattā (non-self)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Tilakkhaṇa (three characteristics)
- → Nicca-saññā (perception of permanence)

**STG:** Contemplation of anicca begins at the initial stages of vipassanā and deepens up to full liberation.

**PRC:** Observing anicca in the body, feelings, and thoughts in real time is the foundation of vipassanā practice. This destroys the illusion of stability (nicca-saññā) and reduces clinging.

---

<a name="term-152"></a>
## 152. Assāda

**Skt.: āsvāda**

**ET:** ā- (toward) + *√svad* (to taste) = "taste," "enjoyment," "gratification."

**DEF:** Gratification, enjoyment, the pleasant aspect of an object. In MN 13 (Mahādukkhakkhandha Sutta), the Buddha explains three aspects of any object or sphere of experience: assāda (gratification), ādīnava (danger), nissaraṇa (escape). Assāda is what makes an object desirable: sensual pleasures bring happiness and joy, form has its attractiveness, etc. However, assāda is incomplete without understanding ādīnava.

**CTX:**
1. **The triad assāda-ādīnava-nissaraṇa** — in MN 13, the Buddha applies this triad to sensual pleasures, form, and feelings.
2. **As the cause of attachment** — the mind seeing only assāda clings to the object, not seeing its danger.
3. **Necessity of understanding** — in AN 4.42, the Buddha says that one must see both assāda and ādīnava to attain liberation.

**QUO:**
> "What, bhikkhus, is the gratification (assāda) of sensual pleasures? Sensual pleasures bring happiness and joy. This is the gratification of sensual pleasures." (MN 13)

**REL:**
- → Ādīnava (danger)
- → Nissaraṇa (escape)
- → Kāma (sensuality)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Sukha (happiness)

**STG:** Understanding assāda develops at all stages. Complete understanding of all three aspects is part of the knowledge of the destruction of taints.

**PRC:** Acknowledging assāda (gratification) of objects is important — denying pleasure is pointless. But one must see its limitation and danger to avoid falling into the trap.

---

<a name="term-153"></a>
## 153. Ādīnava

**Skt.: ādīnava**

**ET:** ā- (toward) + *√dī* (to be dissatisfied, suffer) = "danger," "drawback," "unsatisfactory side."

**DEF:** Danger, drawback, the unsatisfactory side of an object. In MN 13, the Buddha explains ādīnava of sensual pleasures: they are impermanent, full of suffering, subject to change. This is the understanding that even the pleasant contains the seeds of suffering. Seeing ādīnava leads to nibbidā (disenchantment) and virāga (dispassion).

**CTX:**
1. **The triad assāda-ādīnava-nissaraṇa** — in MN 13, ādīnava follows assāda as the second aspect.
2. **As an antidote to attachment** — seeing ādīnava destroys the illusion that the object can bring lasting satisfaction.
3. **Universality** — all conditioned phenomena have ādīnava, even the most subtle and sublime.

**QUO:**
> "What, bhikkhus, is the danger (ādīnava) of sensual pleasures? Sensual pleasures are impermanent, full of suffering, subject to change. This is the danger of sensual pleasures." (MN 13)

**REL:**
- → Assāda (gratification)
- → Nissaraṇa (escape)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Nibbidā (disenchantment)

**STG:** Seeing ādīnava develops at all stages, especially at the stage of vipassanā.

**PRC:** Without understanding ādīnava, a practitioner may become attached even to spiritual experiences (jhānas, states of calm). Seeing danger in all conditioned phenomena motivates the search for the unconditioned.

---

<a name="term-154"></a>
## 154. Nissaraṇa

**Skt.: niḥsaraṇa**

**ET:** nis- (out, away) + *√sṛ* (to flow, move) = "escape," "release," "liberation."

**DEF:** Escape, release, liberation from an object or sphere of experience. In MN 13, the Buddha defines nissaraṇa from sensual pleasures as the removal of desire and passion for them. This is not merely physical avoidance, but an inner liberation through understanding. Nissaraṇa is the third aspect of the triad, showing that there is a way out even from the most attractive or habitual states.

**CTX:**
1. **The triad assāda-ādīnava-nissaraṇa** — in MN 13, nissaraṇa completes the analysis, showing the path to freedom.
2. **As a synonym for nibbāna** — in some contexts, nissaraṇa is used as a synonym for liberation.
3. **Practical escape** — nissaraṇa includes both the inner removal of desire and external methods (avoidance, developing opposite qualities).

**QUO:**
> "What, bhikkhus, is the escape (nissaraṇa) from sensual pleasures? The removal of desire and passion for sensual pleasures — this is the escape from them." (MN 13)

**REL:**
- → Assāda (gratification)
- → Ādīnava (danger)
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna)
- → Virāga (dispassion)
- → Nekkhamma (renunciation)

**STG:** The realization of nissaraṇa is attained at the level of arahatta for each sphere of experience.

**PRC:** This triad provides a complete analysis of any experience: acknowledge its attractiveness, see its danger, find the escape. This is a structured approach to working with attachment.

---

<a name="term-155"></a>
## 155. Subha

**Skt.: śubha**

**ET:** from *√śubh* (to shine, be beautiful) = "beautiful," "attractive."

**DEF:** Beautiful, attractive — the perception (saññā) of the attractiveness of objects, especially the body. In AN 10.60 (Girimānanda Sutta), subha-saññā (perception of attractiveness) is mentioned as one of ten perceptions. The opposite of asubha (unattractive). As long as the mind perceives the body as attractive (subha), kāma-rāga (sensual passion) arises. Overcome through asubha-bhāvanā (contemplation of unattractiveness).

**CTX:**
1. **As a perception** — in AN 10.60, subha-saññā is listed among ten perceptions to be developed or overcome depending on context.
2. **As the cause of passion** — in MN 10, the mind that perceives the body as attractive develops attachment to it.
3. **Overcoming** — in AN 6.102, asubha-saññā is the direct antidote to subha-saññā.

**QUO:**
> "The perception of attractiveness (subha-saññā), when developed and cultivated, brings great fruit and great benefit." (AN 10.60)

**REL:**
- → Asubha (unattractive)
- → Subha-saññā (perception of attractiveness)
- → Kāma-rāga (sensual passion)
- → Asubha-bhāvanā (contemplation of unattractiveness)
- → Saññā (perception)

**STG:** Overcoming subha-saññā is especially important at the beginning and middle stages for weakening sensual passion.

**PRC:** Most people automatically perceive the body (their own and others') as attractive. Conscious development of asubha-saññā helps balance this perception and reduce lust.

---

<a name="term-156"></a>
## 156. Asubha

**Skt.: aśubha**

**ET:** a- (not) + *śubha* (attractive) = "unattractive," "repulsive."

**DEF:** Unattractive, repulsive — perception of the body's unattractiveness. In AN 10.60, asubha-saññā (perception of unattractiveness) is mentioned as one of the ten perceptions. In MN 10, it is part of kāyānupassanā (contemplation of the body) through the contemplation of corpses at various stages of decomposition. A direct antidote to kāma-rāga (sensual passion).

**CTX:**
1. **As a perception** — in AN 10.60, asubha-saññā is recommended for overcoming sensual passion.
2. **In satipaṭṭhāna** — in MN 10, asubha-bhāvanā (contemplation of unattractiveness) is part of the contemplation of the body.
3. **As an antidote** — in AN 6.102: "The perception of unattractiveness (asubha-saññā), bhikkhus, when developed and cultivated, dispels sensual desire (kāmacchanda)."

**QUO:**
> "Furthermore, bhikkhus, as if he saw a corpse thrown in a charnel ground, decomposed, blackened... and he compares this body with it: 'This body has the same nature, it will become like that, it cannot escape it.'" (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Subha (attractive)
- → Asubha-saññā (perception of unattractiveness)
- → Kāmacchanda (sensual desire)
- → Asubha-bhāvanā (contemplation of unattractiveness)
- → Kāyānupassanā (contemplation of body)

**STG:** Asubha-bhāvanā is especially useful at the beginning and middle stages for weakening sensual passion. At higher stages, it is replaced by more subtle practices.

**PRC:** Asubha helps destroy the illusion of the body's attractiveness, which underlies much of sensual desire. However, it is important to practice it in a balanced way to avoid developing aversion towards the body.

---

<a name="term-157"></a>
## 157. Sukha

**Skt.: sukha**

**ET:** su- (good) + *kha* (space, axle) = "good space," "happiness," "pleasure."

**DEF:** Happiness, pleasure, well-being. In the context of vedanā — sukha-vedanā (pleasant feeling). In the context of jhānas — sukha as a factor of the first, second, and third jhānas (in the fourth it is replaced by upekkhā). Kāyika-sukha (bodily pleasure) and cetasika-sukha (mental pleasure) are distinguished. Ultimately, even sukha is impermanent and contains the seeds of dukkha.

**CTX:**
1. **As a feeling** — in SN 36.6, sukha is one of the three types of vedanā.
2. **As a jhāna factor** — in DN 2, sukha is present in the first three jhānas.
3. **Highest happiness** — in Dhp 202, nibbāna is described as the highest happiness (sukhaṃ paramaṃ).
4. **Impermanence** — even sukha is subject to change and therefore ultimately dukkha.

**QUO:**
> "Nibbāna, bhikkhus, is the highest happiness." (Dhp 202)

**REL:**
- → Sukha-vedanā (pleasant feeling)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Jhāna (jhāna)
- → Upekkhā (equanimity)
- → Nibbāna (nibbāna as the highest happiness)

**STG:** Experiencing sukha is possible at all stages. Understanding its impermanence develops through vipassanā.

**PRC:** Buddhism does not deny sukha, but shows its limitation. Even the highest meditative pleasures are impermanent. True happiness — nibbāna — lies beyond conditioned pleasure and pain.

---

<a name="term-158"></a>
## 158. Dukkha (suffering)

**Skt.: duḥkha**

**ET:** du- (bad, difficult) + *kha* (axle, space) = "badly rotating wheel," "difficult space."

**DEF:** Suffering, unsatisfactoriness, difficulty. The first noble truth. Three types: dukkha-dukkhatā (ordinary suffering — pain, grief, etc.), vipariṇāma-dukkhatā (suffering due to change — when the pleasant becomes unpleasant), saṅkhāra-dukkhatā (suffering due to the conditioned nature of all phenomena — even neutral states are unsatisfactory because they are impermanent and impersonal). All conditioned things are dukkha.

**CTX:**
1. **First noble truth** — in SN 56.11, the Buddha proclaims: "This is the noble truth of suffering."
2. **Three types** — in the commentarial tradition, three types of dukkha are described in detail.
3. **Universality** — in SN 22.59: "Whatever is impermanent is suffering."

**QUO:**
> "Birth is suffering, aging is suffering, illness is suffering, death is suffering, sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair are suffering; not getting what one wants is suffering; in brief, the five aggregates subject to clinging are suffering." (SN 56.11)

**REL:**
- → Sukha (happiness)
- → Dukkha-vedanā (unpleasant feeling)
- → Anicca (impermanence)
- → Anattā (non-self)
- → Ariya-sacca (noble truth)

**STG:** Dukkha is fully understood at the level of sotāpatti, but completely overcome only at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Correct understanding of dukkha is not pessimism, but realistic vision. It motivates practice and directs efforts towards true liberation.

---

<a name="term-159"></a>
## 159. Adukkham-asukhā

**Skt.: aduḥkhāsukhā**

**ET:** a- (not) + *dukkha* (suffering) + *a-* (not) + *sukhā* (happiness) = "neither-pain-nor-pleasant," neutral.

**DEF:** Neutral feeling — the third type of vedanā, which is neither pleasant nor unpleasant. In SN 36.1, it is defined as a feeling that is neither sukha nor dukkha. It often goes unnoticed, but is also subject to impermanence and is an object for clinging (especially through ignorance). It is important to be aware of neutral feelings as well, since they too are part of experience.

**CTX:**
1. **Three types of feelings** — in SN 36.1: "There are, bhikkhus, three kinds of feeling: pleasant, unpleasant, neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant."
2. **As an object of mindfulness** — in MN 10, the practitioner is aware: "When a neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant feeling is felt, he understands: 'I feel a neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant feeling.'"
3. **Also impermanent** — adukkham-asukhā is also subject to anicca and is part of dukkha in the sense of saṅkhāra-dukkhatā.

**QUO:**
> "When a neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant feeling is felt, he understands: 'I feel a neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant feeling.'" (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Sukha-vedanā (pleasant feeling)
- → Dukkha-vedanā (unpleasant feeling)
- → Upekkhā (equanimity — close but not identical)

**STG:** Awareness of adukkham-asukhā develops at all stages of satipaṭṭhāna practice.

**PRC:** Neutral feelings are often ignored, but they constitute a significant part of experience. Being aware of them develops the fullness of attention and shows that even the "neutral" is impermanent.

---

<a name="term-160"></a>
## 160. Upekkhā (as feeling)

**Skt.: upekṣā**

**ET:** upa- (toward, near) + *√ikṣ* (to see) = "looking on from the side," "equanimity."

**DEF:** Equanimity — in the context of feelings, it is not a feeling itself but an attitude towards feelings. However, sometimes upekkhā is mentioned as a feeling, especially in the context of the fourth jhāna, where it replaces sukha. It is a balanced, equanimous state, free from attraction to the pleasant and repulsion from the unpleasant. Not indifference but perfect equilibrium.

**CTX:**
1. **As a factor of the fourth jhāna** — in DN 2: "With the abandoning of pleasure and pain... he enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna, which has neither-pain-nor-pleasure and purity of mindfulness due to equanimity (upekkhā)."
2. **As a brahmavihāra** — upekkhā as a divine abode is equanimity towards all beings.
3. **As a factor of awakening** — upekkhā is the seventh factor of awakening (bojjhaṅga).

**QUO:**
> "With the abandoning of pleasure and pain... he enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna, which has neither-pain-nor-pleasure and purity of mindfulness due to equanimity." (DN 2)

**REL:**
- → Sukha (happiness)
- → Dukkha (suffering)
- → Adukkham-asukhā (neutral feeling)
- → Jhāna (jhāna)
- → Brahmavihāra (divine abodes)
- → Bojjhaṅga (factors of awakening)

**STG:** Upekkhā develops at all stages. Complete perfection is at the level of arahatta.

**PRC:** Upekkhā allows one to observe all phenomena — pleasant, unpleasant, neutral — with equal impartiality. This is the key to ceasing reaction to experience.

<a name="term-161"></a>
## 161. Āgama

**Skt.: āgama**

**ET:** ā- (toward) + *√gam* (to go) = "coming," "arrival," "tradition," "collection of teachings."

**DEF:** Collection of teachings, tradition, canonical text. In the Pāli tradition, the term is sometimes used as a synonym for Nikāya (collection of suttas). In northern Buddhism (Sanskrit tradition), Āgama corresponds to the Pāli Nikāyas: Dīrgha-āgama (Long Discourses), Madhyama-āgama (Middle Discourses), Saṃyukta-āgama (Connected Discourses), Ekottara-āgama (Numerical Discourses). In Pāli commentaries, it sometimes appears as a designation for canonical texts. Emphasizes the idea of transmission of the teaching from teacher to student.

**CTX:**
1. **As a synonym for Nikāya** — in commentaries on the Pāli Canon, the term āgama is sometimes used for collections of suttas.
2. **In the northern tradition** — four Āgamas are preserved in Chinese translation and correspond to the four main Nikāyas of the Pāli Canon.
3. **As transmission of the teaching** — āgama implies an unbroken oral transmission of the teaching from teacher to student.

**QUO:**
> "These four Āgamas, bhikkhus, are well proclaimed by me and made known." (from the commentarial tradition)

**REL:**
- → Nikāya (collection of suttas)
- → Sutta (discourse)
- → Tipiṭaka (Three Baskets)
- → Pariyatti (study)
- → Anussava (oral tradition)

**STG:** Study of āgama (canonical texts) is part of pariyatti (theoretical study) at all stages.

**PRC:** Āgama represents an authoritative source of the teaching that should be studied, practiced, and transmitted. Without studying the texts, practice can go astray.

---

<a name="term-162"></a>
## 162. Mātikā

**Skt.: mātṛkā**

**ET:** from *mātṛ* (mother) = "matrix," "foundation," "list."

**DEF:** Matrix or list of the fundamental points of the teaching. In the Abhidhamma, mātikā is a list of classifications of dhammas that are then elaborated in detail. In the Vinaya, it refers to the list of rules. It can also mean a table of contents or a schema of the teaching. Mātikā serves as a mnemonic device for memorizing and systematizing the teaching. It is the "mother" of all subsequent elaborations.

**CTX:**
1. **In the Abhidhamma** — at the beginning of the Abhidhamma-piṭaka, a mātikā is given listing all dhammas by category (e.g., kusala dhamma, akusala dhamma, abyākata dhamma).
2. **In the Vinaya** — mātikā may refer to the list of rules in the Pātimokkha.
3. **As a mnemonic device** — in the oral tradition, mātikā helped memorize and systematize the vast teaching.

**QUO:**
> "Bhikkhus, I will teach you the Dhamma in the form of a matrix (mātikā)." (from the commentarial tradition)

**REL:**
- → Abhidhamma (higher Dhamma)
- → Vinaya (discipline)
- → Pātimokkha (monastic code)
- → Saṅgīti (council, recitation)

**STG:** Study of mātikā is useful for systematic understanding of the teaching, especially at the stage of pariyatti.

**PRC:** Mātikā provides a structured approach to studying the Dhamma. It is a map that helps navigate the vast teaching.

---

<a name="term-163"></a>
## 163. Tipiṭaka

**Skt.: Tripiṭaka**

**ET:** ti/tri (three) + *piṭaka* (basket) = "three baskets."

**DEF:** Three Baskets — the complete canon of Buddhist scriptures. Consists of: (1) Vinaya-piṭaka (Basket of Discipline) — rules for the monastic community; (2) Sutta-piṭaka (Basket of Discourses) — sermons and talks of the Buddha; (3) Abhidhamma-piṭaka (Basket of Higher Dhamma) — philosophical and psychological analysis of the teaching. The name "baskets" comes from the fact that texts were originally stored in woven baskets, or that these are "collections" (piṭaka also means "collection").

**CTX:**
1. **Structure of the Canon** — in the Pāli tradition, the Tipiṭaka is divided into three parts, each with its own internal structure.
2. **As an object of study** — in AN 3.85, the Buddha speaks of the importance of studying, practicing, and realizing the Dhamma, which corresponds to the three piṭakas.
3. **Transmission** — the Tipiṭaka was transmitted orally for several centuries until first committed to writing in the 1st century BCE in Sri Lanka.

**QUO:**
> "One who sees the Dhamma sees me; one who sees me sees the Dhamma." (SN 22.87)

**REL:**
- → Vinaya-piṭaka (Basket of Discipline)
- → Sutta-piṭaka (Basket of Discourses)
- → Abhidhamma-piṭaka (Basket of Higher Dhamma)
- → Pariyatti (study)
- → Saṅgīti (council)

**STG:** Study of the Tipiṭaka is part of pariyatti at all stages. However, for liberation, not only study but also practice (paṭipatti) and realization (paṭivedha) are necessary.

**PRC:** The Tipiṭaka is the complete collection of the Buddha's teachings. Studying all three baskets gives a holistic understanding of the path: ethics (Vinaya), practice (Sutta), and wisdom (Abhidhamma).

---

<a name="term-164"></a>
## 164. Sutta

**Skt.: sūtra**

**ET:** from *√siv* (to sew) = "thread," "thread of teaching," discourse.

**DEF:** Discourse, sermon, talk of the Buddha or his chief disciples. The Sutta-piṭaka contains over 10,000 suttas. A sutta usually has a specific structure: introduction (thus have I heard), the main teaching, and conclusion. They serve as practical guidance for realizing the Dhamma. They are not philosophical treatises but are aimed at the cessation of suffering.

**CTX:**
1. **As part of the Tipiṭaka** — the Sutta-piṭaka is divided into five Nikāyas: Dīgha, Majjhima, Saṃyutta, Aṅguttara, Khuddaka.
2. **Structure** — most suttas begin with "Thus have I heard" (evaṃ me sutaṃ), indicating oral transmission.
3. **Practical orientation** — in MN 22 (Alagaddūpama Sutta), the Buddha says that the Dhamma is like a raft for crossing the river, not to be carried on one's head.

**QUO:**
> "Thus have I heard. On one occasion the Blessed One was dwelling at Sāvatthī, in Jeta's Grove, in Anāthapiṇḍika's monastery..." (standard sutta opening)

**REL:**
- → Sutta-piṭaka (Basket of Discourses)
- → Nikāya (collection of suttas)
- → Dīgha-nikāya, Majjhima-nikāya, Saṃyutta-nikāya, Aṅguttara-nikāya, Khuddaka-nikāya
- → Pariyatti (study)
- → Desanā (sermon)

**STG:** Study of suttas is important at all stages. They provide both theoretical understanding and practical instructions.

**PRC:** Suttas are the direct word of the Buddha. Studying and applying them in practice is a way to "meet" the teaching directly.

---

<a name="term-165"></a>
## 165. Vinaya

**Skt.: vinaya**

**ET:** vi- (separation) + *√nī* (to lead) = "discipline," "leading away," "removal [of faults]."

**DEF:** Discipline, monastic code. The Vinaya-piṭaka contains rules for the monastic community (bhikkhu and bhikkhunī). It includes the Pātimokkha (the basic 227 rules for bhikkhus), as well as the origin stories of each rule, procedures for resolving conflicts, and rules for communal harmony. The purpose of the Vinaya is to create external conditions for practice and to help remove defilements through control of body and speech.

**CTX:**
1. **As part of the Tipiṭaka** — the Vinaya-piṭaka is divided into three parts: Suttavibhaṅga (analysis of rules), Khandhaka (sections), Parivāra (appendixes).
2. **For the monastic community** — Vinaya regulates the life of the Saṅgha, ensuring harmony and purity of conduct.
3. **As a foundation for samādhi** — in AN 11.1, the Buddha says that morality (including Vinaya) leads to freedom from remorse, which in turn leads to joy, happiness, and concentration.

**QUO:**
> "Vinaya, bhikkhus, is the life of the Teaching. As long as Vinaya is preserved, the Teaching is preserved." (from the commentaries)

**REL:**
- → Vinaya-piṭaka (Basket of Discipline)
- → Pātimokkha (monastic code)
- → Saṅgha (community)
- → Sīla (morality)
- → Āpatti (offence)

**STG:** Following the Vinaya is mandatory for monks at all stages. For laypeople, there are less strict rules (pañca-sīla).

**PRC:** Vinaya creates an external structure that supports internal practice. Without discipline, the mind remains restless and unable to develop deep concentration.

---

<a name="term-166"></a>
## 166. Abhidhamma

**Skt.: abhidharma**

**ET:** abhi- (higher, special) + *dhamma* (teaching, phenomenon) = "higher Dhamma," "special teaching."

**DEF:** Higher Dhamma — a systematic, analytical study of the teaching. The Abhidhamma-piṭaka contains seven books that classify all phenomena (dhammas) into ultimate components, analyze their relationships, and reveal subtle aspects of the teaching. It is not a new doctrine but a methodological tool for deep understanding of the Suttas. It is traditionally held that the Abhidhamma was taught by the Buddha in the world of gods (devaloka).

**CTX:**
1. **As part of the Tipiṭaka** — the Abhidhamma-piṭaka consists of seven books: Dhammasaṅgaṇī, Vibhaṅga, Dhātukathā, Puggalapaññatti, Kathāvatthu, Yamaka, Paṭṭhāna.
2. **Analytical method** — Abhidhamma analyses experience into ultimate, impersonal constituents (dhammas), showing their characteristics, functions, manifestations, and proximate causes.
3. **For deep understanding** — in the commentaries, it is said that the Abhidhamma is intended for those whose minds are sharp as a razor.

**QUO:**
> "The Abhidhamma, bhikkhus, is profound and difficult to understand." (from the commentaries)

**REL:**
- → Abhidhamma-piṭaka (Basket of Higher Dhamma)
- → Dhamma (phenomenon)
- → Paramattha-dhamma (ultimate reality)
- → Paññatti (concept)
- → Pariyatti (study)

**STG:** Study of the Abhidhamma is especially useful at advanced stages for deepening understanding. However, for liberation, direct seeing, not just intellectual knowledge, is necessary.

**PRC:** The Abhidhamma provides a precise language for describing experience and a map of reality that supports vipassanā practice. However, it is important not to get stuck in concepts but to use them as tools for direct seeing.

<a name="term-167"></a>
## 167. Nikāya

**Skt.: nikāya**

**ET:** from *√nī* (to lead) + *kāya* (body, collection) = "collection," "assemblage."

**DEF:** Collection, collection of suttas. In the Pāli Canon, the Sutta-piṭaka is divided into five Nikāyas: Dīgha-nikāya (Collection of Long Discourses), Majjhima-nikāya (Collection of Middle-Length Discourses), Saṃyutta-nikāya (Collection of Connected Discourses), Aṅguttara-nikāya (Collection of Numerical Discourses), Khuddaka-nikāya (Collection of Minor Texts). It may also refer to a monastic lineage or school.

**CTX:**
1. **Five Nikāyas** — these form the main division of the Sutta-piṭaka in the Pāli tradition.
2. **Monastic nikāyas** — in later periods, nikāya came to denote monastic lineages or schools (e.g., Mahāvihāra-nikāya).
3. **As a collection** — nikāya literally means "collection" or "assemblage" of texts.

**QUO:**
> "These five Nikāyas, bhikkhus, are well proclaimed by me." (from the commentaries)

**REL:**
- → Sutta-piṭaka (Basket of Discourses)
- → Dīgha-nikāya, Majjhima-nikāya, Saṃyutta-nikāya, Aṅguttara-nikāya, Khuddaka-nikāya
- → Āgama (collections in the northern tradition)
- → Pariyatti (study)

**STG:** Study of the Nikāyas is part of pariyatti at all stages.

**PRC:** Different Nikāyas present the teaching in different formats: systematically (Saṃyutta), thematically (Aṅguttara), in detail (Dīgha), concisely (Majjhima). This allows studying the Dhamma from different angles.

<a name="term-168"></a>
## 168. Pariyatti

**Skt.: pariyatti**

**ET:** pari- (around, completeness) + *√i* (to go) = "study," "theoretical knowledge."

**DEF:** Study of the teaching, theoretical knowledge of the Dhamma. Together with paṭipatti (practice) and paṭivedha (realization), it constitutes the three aspects of mastering the Dhamma. Pariyatti includes studying the Tipiṭaka, memorizing texts, and understanding doctrines. Without pariyatti, practice may be misguided; without practice, pariyatti is futile.

**CTX:**
1. **Three aspects** — in the commentarial tradition: pariyatti (study), paṭipatti (practice), paṭivedha (realization).
2. **As foundation** — in AN 3.20, the Buddha speaks of the importance of study, practice, and realization.
3. **Danger of only pariyatti** — in SN 20.7, the Buddha warns that knowing many suttas without practice is like a cowherd who counts others' cows but gets no milk from them.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these three kinds of training. What three? Training in higher virtue, training in higher mind, training in higher wisdom." (AN 3.20)

**REL:**
- → Paṭipatti (practice)
- → Paṭivedha (realization)
- → Tipiṭaka (Three Baskets)
- → Suta-mayā paññā (wisdom born of learning)

**STG:** Pariyatti is important at all stages, but especially at the beginning. At higher stages, direct knowledge replaces the need for theoretical study.

**PRC:** Balance between theory and practice is important. Blind practice without understanding can lead to a dead end, while purely theoretical study without practice will not lead to liberation.

<a name="term-169"></a>
## 169. Desanā

**Skt.: deśanā**

**ET:** from *√diś* (to point, show) = "sermon," "instruction," "pointing out."

**DEF:** Sermon, instruction, pointing out the path. In MN 141 (Saccavibhaṅga Sutta), the Buddha gives desanā on the four noble truths. Desanā is the act of transmitting the Dhamma from teacher to student. It includes not only words but also example and the energy of transmission. The Buddha is known as the "unsurpassed teacher" (anuttaro purisadammasārathi).

**CTX:**
1. **Sermon of Dhamma** — in MN 26 (Ariyapariyesanā Sutta), after enlightenment, the Buddha decides to give desanā to those with little dust in their eyes.
2. **Skill in teaching** — in AN 3.67, the Buddha says that a sermon should be timely, truthful, gentle, beneficial, and with kind intention.
3. **Different kinds of desanā** — may be formal (to an assembly) or informal (personal guidance).

**QUO:**
> "I will teach you the Dhamma: 'This is suffering, this is the origin of suffering, this is the cessation of suffering, this is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'" (SN 56.11)

**REL:**
- → Dhammadesanā (sermon of Dhamma)
- → Ācariya (teacher)
- → Ovāda (admonition)
- → Anusāsana (instruction)
- → Satthā (teacher)

**STG:** Receiving desanā is important at all stages. Even arahants may need reminders and clarification.

**PRC:** Desanā is the bridge between the understanding of the teacher and the student. Without a competent teacher, it is easy to get lost on the path.

<a name="term-170"></a>
## 170. Ācariya

**Skt.: ācārya**

**ET:** from *√car* (to move, behave) = "one who teaches conduct," "teacher."

**DEF:** Teacher, instructor. In the Vinaya, a distinction is made between ācariya (teacher who gives instruction in the Dhamma) and upajjhāya (preceptor who conducts ordination). The ācariya plays a crucial role in the transmission of the teaching. The Buddha is the supreme ācariya. The relationship between ācariya and student is based on respect, gratitude, and obedience.

**CTX:**
1. **In the monastic tradition** — every newly ordained monk must have an ācariya or upajjhāya.
2. **Qualities of a teacher** — in MN 107 (Gaṇakamoggallāna Sutta), the Buddha describes the qualities of a good teacher.
3. **Respect for the teacher** — in DN 31 (Siṅgālovāda Sutta), the proper attitude of a student towards a teacher is discussed.

**QUO:**
> "A teacher, bhikkhus, is worthy of respect, and a student should serve the teacher." (from the Vinaya)

**REL:**
- → Upajjhāya (preceptor)
- → Satthā (teacher)
- → Kalyāṇa-mitta (good friend)
- → Sissa (student)
- → Vinaya (discipline)

**STG:** Having a competent ācariya is especially important at the early stages. At higher stages, the student becomes independent but still maintains respect for the teacher.

**PRC:** Without a competent teacher, it is easy to develop wrong views and practices. The ācariya points out mistakes and guides one on the right path.

<a name="term-171"></a>
## 171. Sekha

**Skt.: śaikṣa**

**ET:** from *√śikṣ* (to learn) = "learner," "one in training."

**DEF:** One-in-training — one who has attained one of the first three stages of awakening (sotāpatti, sakadāgāmitā, anāgāmitā) and continues practice towards full liberation. In AN 3.85 (Sekha Sutta), it is described as one who understands the Four Noble Truths but has not yet fully realized them. The sekha still has residual taints (āsava) and needs further training and practice.

**CTX:**
1. **Three stages of learners** — sekha includes the sotāpanna (stream-enterer), sakadāgāmī (once-returner), and anāgāmī (non-returner). All are on the path to arahatta.
2. **Contrast with asekha** — in AN 3.85, the Buddha distinguishes between sekha (one-in-training) and asekha (one beyond training — an arahant).
3. **Practice of a sekha** — the sekha continues to develop morality (sīla), concentration (samādhi), and wisdom (paññā) to remove the remaining defilements.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is one-in-training? Here a bhikkhu is a disciple of the Noble Ones, possessing right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration. This is called one-in-training." (AN 3.85)

**REL:**
- → Asekha (one beyond training)
- → Sotāpanna, Sakadāgāmī, Anāgāmī (three stages of learners)
- → Ariya-sāvaka (noble disciple)
- → Āsava (taints)
- → Paṭipatti (practice)

**STG:** Sekha is the stage after attaining sotāpatti and before arahatta. The practitioner remains a sekha until all defilements are completely destroyed.

**PRC:** Understanding that the path consists of stages helps the practitioner not to become complacent after initial attainments. The sekha must continue practice with diligence until becoming an asekha.

<a name="term-172"></a>
## 172. Asekha

**Skt.: aśaikṣa**

**ET:** a- (not) + *sekha* (learner, one-in-training) = "one beyond training."

**DEF:** One who has completed training — an arahant. Contrasted with sekha (one-in-training). The arahant no longer needs training because all taints (āsava) are destroyed and final liberation (vimutti) has been attained. In AN 3.85 (Sekha Sutta), asekha is described as one who has fully realized the Four Noble Truths and has nothing further to practice.

**CTX:**
1. **As a synonym for arahant** — in AN 3.85, asekha is defined as one who possesses unsurpassed liberation of mind (anuttarā cetovimutti) and unsurpassed liberation through wisdom (anuttarā paññāvimutti).
2. **Completion of the path** — asekha is the final goal of the Buddhist path. He no longer creates new kamma and is not reborn.
3. **Qualities of asekha** — in SN 22.58, the qualities of an arahant are listed: destruction of lust, hatred, and delusion; absence of attachment to the five aggregates; full understanding of the four noble truths.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is one beyond training? Here a bhikkhu is an arahant, whose taints are destroyed, who has lived the holy life, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, reached his own goal, utterly destroyed the fetters of existence, liberated through final knowledge. This is called one beyond training." (AN 3.85)

**REL:**
- → Sekha (one-in-training)
- → Arahant (arahant)
- → Āsavānaṃ khaya (destruction of taints)
- → Vimutti (liberation)
- → Paṭivedha (realization)

**STG:** Asekha is the fourth and highest stage of awakening (arahatta). The preceding stages — sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī — are all sekha.

**PRC:** Asekha is living proof that liberation is attainable. His example inspires sekha to continue practice until the path is completed.

<a name="term-173"></a>
## 173. Paññatti

**Skt.: prajñapti**

**ET:** from *√jñā* (to know) with prefix *pa-* = "designation," "concept," "conventional name."

**DEF:** Designation, concept, conventional reality. In the Abhidhamma, it is contrasted with paramattha-dhamma (ultimate reality). Paññatti are conceptual constructions used for communication and thinking: "person," "table," "river," "self." They have no own-nature (sabhāva) and exist only as mental designations. In the Canon (MN 18), the Buddha shows that even concepts such as "person" (puggala) are merely conventional designations.

**CTX:**
1. **In the Abhidhamma** — in the Abhidhamma-piṭaka, two kinds of reality are distinguished: paramattha-dhamma (ultimate realities — consciousness, mental factors, matter, nibbāna) and paññatti (concepts).
2. **As a tool for communication** — in MN 18 (Madhupiṇḍika Sutta), the Buddha explains that designations are necessary for communication, but the wise do not cling to them.
3. **Danger of identification** — attachment to paññatti as reality leads to diṭṭhi (views) and suffering.

**QUO:**
> "Bhikkhus, I will teach you the Dhamma — the simile of the honey-ball. Listen carefully. ... So too, bhikkhus, when a monk does not identify with, welcome, or cling to these six bases for contact, then there is no basis for the arising of suffering." (MN 18)

**REL:**
- → Paramattha-dhamma (ultimate reality)
- → Sabhāva (own-nature)
- → Diṭṭhi (view)
- → Puggala (person)
- → Abhidhamma (higher Dhamma)

**STG:** Understanding the difference between paññatti and paramattha-dhamma is important at the study stage (pariyatti) and especially in vipassanā practice, where one needs to see paramattha-dhamma directly.

**PRC:** The practitioner learns to use concepts as tools without mistaking them for reality. This helps destroy attachment to "self" and other concepts, leading to direct seeing of reality.

---

<a name="term-174"></a>
## 174. Paramattha-dhamma

**Skt.: paramārtha-dharma**

**ET:** parama (highest, ultimate) + *attha* (meaning, reality) + *dhamma* (phenomenon) = "phenomenon of ultimate reality."

**DEF:** Ultimate realities — phenomena that exist by their own-nature (sabhāva), not as mere designations. In the Abhidhamma, four categories of paramattha-dhamma are distinguished: citta (consciousness), cetasika (mental factors), rūpa (matter), nibbāna (nibbāna). These phenomena can be directly known through insight (vipassanā). They are the objects of direct knowledge (yathābhūtañāṇadassana).

**CTX:**
1. **Four categories** — in the Abhidhamma-piṭaka (Dhammasaṅgaṇī), all paramattha-dhamma are systematized. Citta (89 or 121 types of consciousness), cetasika (52 mental factors), rūpa (28 types of matter), nibbāna (the unconditioned).
2. **Direct seeing** — in SN 22.95 (Puppha Sutta), the Buddha speaks of the need to see the five aggregates as they are (yathābhūta), i.e., as paramattha-dhamma.
3. **Contrast with paññatti** — paramattha-dhamma is what actually exists, in contrast to concepts (paññatti).

**QUO:**
> "One who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma; one who sees the Dhamma sees dependent origination." (MN 28)

**REL:**
- → Paññatti (concept)
- → Sabhāva (own-nature)
- → Citta, Cetasika, Rūpa, Nibbāna (four paramattha-dhamma)
- → Yathābhūtañāṇadassana (seeing as it is)
- → Abhidhamma (higher Dhamma)

**STG:** Direct knowledge of paramattha-dhamma begins at the stage of vipassanā and culminates at the level of arahatta. Study of paramattha-dhamma is useful at the stage of pariyatti.

**PRC:** Vipassanā practice is directed at directly seeing paramattha-dhamma — momentary, impersonal processes of consciousness, mental factors, and matter. This destroys the illusion of a permanent "self" and leads to liberation.

---

<a name="term-175"></a>
## 175. Karuṇā

**Skt.: karuṇā**

**ET:** from *√kṛ* (to do, act) or *√kar* (to pity) = "compassion," "pity."

**DEF:** Compassion — the second divine abode (brahmavihāra). The wish that all beings be free from suffering (dukkha). In AN 4.125 (Karuṇā Sutta), it is described as a boundless state to be developed towards all beings without exception. Karuṇā is contrasted with cruelty (vihiṃsā) and is the antidote to hatred (dosa).

**CTX:**
1. **As a brahmavihāra** — in DN 13 (Tevijja Sutta), karuṇā is listed among the four divine abodes together with mettā, muditā, and upekkhā. These states lead to rebirth in the Brahma-world.
2. **Boundless compassion** — in MN 7 (Vatthūpama Sutta), the Buddha speaks of developing compassion that has no limits, like a mighty conch sounding in all directions.
3. **Practice of karuṇā-bhāvanā** — in AN 11.15, meditation on compassion is described: "May all beings be free from enmity, may all beings be free from suffering..."
4. **Connection with wisdom** — in SN 46.54, karuṇā is mentioned as a factor that supports the development of insight (vipassanā).

**QUO:**
> "Bhikkhus, there is one way for rebirth in the Brahma-world. What is that? The development of boundless compassion." (AN 4.125)

**REL:**
- → Brahmavihāra (divine abodes)
- → Mettā (loving-kindness)
- → Muditā (sympathetic joy)
- → Upekkhā (equanimity)
- → Dosa (hatred)
- → Vihiṃsā (cruelty)

**STG:** Karuṇā develops at all stages of practice. It is especially important for overcoming hatred and developing a noble heart.

**PRC:** The practice of karuṇā-bhāvanā transforms one's relationship to suffering — both one's own and that of others. It is not sentimental pity but an active wish for liberation from suffering, grounded in wisdom.

---

<a name="term-176"></a>
## 176. Muditā

**Skt.: muditā**

**ET:** from *√mud* (to rejoice) = "sympathetic joy," "rejoicing in others' good fortune."

**DEF:** Sympathetic joy — the third divine abode (brahmavihāra). Joy over the success, well-being, and happiness of other beings. In AN 4.125, it is described as a boundless state to be developed towards all beings without exception. Muditā is the opposite of envy (issā) and discontent (arati).

**CTX:**
1. **As a brahmavihāra** — in DN 13, muditā is one of the four divine states, a joy free from comparison and competition.
2. **Practice of muditā-bhāvanā** — meditation includes phrases such as: "May all beings not be deprived of their good fortune..."
3. **Distinction from pīti** — muditā differs from pīti (rapture as a factor of awakening): pīti is internal joy from practice, while muditā is joy for others.

**QUO:**
> "There is, bhikkhus, a way for rebirth in the Brahma world. What is it? The development of boundless sympathetic joy." (AN 4.125)

**REL:**
- → Brahmavihāra (divine abodes)
- → Mettā (loving-kindness)
- → Karuṇā (compassion)
- → Upekkhā (equanimity)
- → Issā (envy)
- → Arati (discontent)

**STG:** Muditā is developed at all stages. It is especially useful for overcoming envy and comparison with others.

**PRC:** Muditā destroys the habit of envying others' success. By developing sincere joy for others, the practitioner creates a state of openness and generosity in the mind, which contributes to one's own happiness.

---

<a name="term-177"></a>
## 177. Brahmavihāra

**Skt.: brahmavihāra**

**ET:** brahma (highest, sacred) + *vihāra* (abiding, dwelling) = "divine abidings," "sublime states."

**DEF:** Four divine abodes: mettā (loving-kindness), karuṇā (compassion), muditā (sympathetic joy), upekkhā (equanimity). In DN 13 (Tevijja Sutta), they are described as the path to union with Brahma (brahmasahavyatā). These states are called "divine" because they are pure, sublime, and lead to favorable rebirths. However, in Buddhism, they are not the final goal but serve as a foundation for developing insight (vipassanā).

**CTX:**
1. **Four states** — in AN 4.125, each brahmavihāra and its development is described in detail.
2. **As a foundation for insight** — in SN 46.54, it is said that brahmavihāras, when developed, can serve as a basis for developing the factors of awakening (bojjhaṅga).
3. **Boundless (appamaññā)** — brahmavihāras are also called "boundless states" because they are extended to all beings without exception.

**QUO:**
> "These four divine abidings, bhikkhus, when developed and cultivated, yield great fruit and great benefit." (AN 4.125)

**REL:**
- → Mettā, Karuṇā, Muditā, Upekkhā
- → Appamaññā (boundless states)
- → Brahmacariya (holy life)

**STG:** Brahmavihāras are developed at all stages. They are especially important for lay practitioners and serve as a basis for morality and concentration.

**PRC:** Brahmavihāras are heart practices that transform emotional reactions. They create a foundation for a calm and concentrated mind necessary for vipassanā. However, it is important to remember that even the highest rebirths in Brahma worlds remain part of saṃsāra, so brahmavihāras must be combined with the development of wisdom.

<a name="term-178"></a>
## 178. Upāsaka / Upāsikā

**Skt.: upāsaka / upāsikā**

**ET:** upa- (near) + *√ās* (to sit) = "one who sits near," "follower," a layman / laywoman.

**DEF:** Lay followers who have taken refuge in the Three Jewels (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha) and observe the five precepts (pañca-sīla). In DN 16 (Mahāparinibbāna Sutta), it is described that lay people can attain sotāpatti and higher. Upāsaka (masc.) and upāsikā (fem.) are an important part of the Buddhist community, providing material support to the monastic saṅgha.

**CTX:**
1. **Taking refuge** — in MN 56 (Upāli Sutta), it is described how a lay person takes refuge by reciting the formula: "Buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi, Dhammaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi, Saṅghaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi."
2. **Attaining noble stages** — in SN 55.24, it is said that lay people possessing four qualities (unwavering faith in Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, and morality pleasing to the Noble Ones) are stream-enterers (sotāpanna).

**QUO:**
> "Here, householder, a noble disciple possesses unwavering faith in the Buddha... in the Dhamma... in the Saṅgha... He possesses morality pleasing to the Noble Ones — unbroken, untarnished... This, householder, is called a noble disciple who has entered the stream." (SN 55.24)

**REL:**
- → Pañca-sīla (five precepts)
- → Sotāpanna (stream-enterer)
- → Saṅgha (community)
- → Dāna (generosity)
- → Gahattha (householder)

**STG:** Lay people can attain all four stages of awakening, including arahatta, though this is rare. Most lay people attain sotāpatti or sakadāgāmitā.

<a name="term-179"></a>
## 179. Pāramī

**Skt.: pāramitā**

**ET:** pāra (the other shore) + *√i* (to go) = "perfection," "that which leads to the other shore [of saṃsāra]."

**DEF:** Perfections — qualities developed by a bodhisatta (future Buddha) over many lives to attain full awakening (sammā-sambodhi). In the Theravāda tradition, ten pāramī are usually listed: dāna (generosity), sīla (morality), nekkhamma (renunciation), paññā (wisdom), viriya (energy), khanti (patience), sacca (truthfulness), adhiṭṭhāna (determination), mettā (loving-kindness), upekkhā (equanimity).

**QUO:**
> "Just as all rivers flow into the ocean, so all good qualities flow into the six pāramī." (from commentaries)

**REL:**
- → Bodhisatta (bodhisatta)
- → Dāna, Sīla, Nekkhamma, Paññā, Viriya, Khanti, Sacca, Adhiṭṭhāna, Mettā, Upekkhā
- → Jātaka (birth stories)
- → Sammā-sambodhi (full awakening)

## 180. Vijjā

**Skt.: vidyā**

**ET:** from *√vid* (to know) = "knowledge," especially spiritual knowledge that destroys ignorance (avijjā).

**DEF:** Knowledge — especially spiritual knowledge that destroys ignorance. In DN 2 (Sāmaññaphala Sutta), three kinds of vijjā (tevijjā) are described: (1) knowledge of past lives (pubbe-nivāsānussati-ñāṇa), (2) knowledge of death and rebirth of beings (cutūpapāta-ñāṇa), (3) knowledge of the destruction of taints (āsavānaṃ khaya-ñāṇa).

**QUO:**

## 181. Ñāṇa

**Skt.: jñāna**

**ET:** from *√ñā* (to know) = "knowledge," "cognition," especially direct, experiential knowledge.

**DEF:** Knowledge, cognition — especially in the context of direct, experiential understanding. In SN 12.70, ñāṇa is defined as knowledge of dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda). It is not conceptual knowledge but direct vision (dassana) of the nature of phenomena.

**QUO:**
> "Who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma; who sees the Dhamma sees dependent origination." (SN 12.70)

**REL:**
- → Paññā (wisdom)
- → Dassana (vision)
- → Vipassanā-ñāṇa (knowledge of insight)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Yathābhūtañāṇadassana (seeing things as they are)

## 182. Pariññā

**Skt.: parijñā**

**ET:** pari- (completely, around) + *√ñā* (to know) = "full understanding," "comprehensive knowledge."

**DEF:** Full, comprehensive understanding that leads to the cessation of attachment. In SN 56.11, pariññā is mentioned as the task regarding the first noble truth: "This noble truth of suffering must be fully understood (pariññeyya)."

**QUO:**

**REL:**

<a name="term-183"></a>
## 183. Dassana

**Skt.: darśana**

**ET:** from *√dṛś* (to see) = "vision," "direct experience," "intuitive understanding."

**DEF:** Vision, direct experience, intuitive understanding. In MN 2 (Sabbāsava Sutta), dassana is mentioned as the first method for eliminating taints (āsava): some taints should be abandoned through seeing (dassana). Dassana is not physical sight but spiritual insight, direct knowledge of the nature of phenomena.

**QUO:**
> "When, bhikkhus, a noble disciple understands impermanence, understands suffering, understands non-self, this is called seeing (dassana)." (MN 2)

**REL:**
- → Ñāṇa (knowledge)
- → Dhammacakkhu (eye of Dhamma)
- → Yathābhūtañāṇadassana (seeing as it is)
- → Āsava (taints)
- → Vipassanā (insight)

<a name="term-184"></a>
## 184. Ākāsa-dhātu

**Skt.: ākāśa-dhātu**

**ET:** ākāsa (space, ether) + *dhātu* (element) = "space element."

**DEF:** The space element — one of the six elements (cha dhātu) together with earth, water, fire, air, and consciousness. In MN 140 (Dhātu-vibhaṅga Sutta), the Buddha analyses ākāsa-dhātu as internal space (e.g., space in ears, nostrils) and external space.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the space element? The space element may be internal or external. What is the internal space element? That which is internal, belonging to oneself, is space, spatial, hollow..." (MN 140)

**REL:**
- → Dhātu (element)
- → Paṭhavī-dhātu, Āpo-dhātu, Tejo-dhātu, Vāyo-dhātu, Viññāṇa-dhātu
- → Ākāsānañcāyatana (sphere of boundless space)
- → Suññatā (emptiness)
- → Arūpa-jhāna (formless jhāna)

<a name="term-185"></a>
## 185. Vipāka

**Skt.: vipāka**

**ET:** vi- (separation, change) + *√pac* (to cook, ripen) = "fruit," "result," "ripened consequence."

**DEF:** The fruit or result of kamma (kamma), which ripens as pleasant or unpleasant experiences. In SN 36.21, vipāka that ripen in this life (diṭṭhadhammavedanīya), in the next life (upapajjavedanīya), or in subsequent lives (aparāpariyavedanīya) are distinguished.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, three kinds of feeling arising from kamma: feeling arising from kamma that ripens in this life; feeling arising from kamma that ripens in the next life; feeling arising from kamma that ripens in subsequent lives." (SN 36.21)

**REL:**
- → Kamma (kamma)
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Kusala-kamma (wholesome kamma)
- → Akusala-kamma (unwholesome kamma)
- → Phala (fruit)

---

<a name="term-186"></a>
## 186. Puñña

**Skt.: puṇya**

**ET:** from *√puñ* (to purify, bring benefit) = "merit," "wholesome action," "purifying action."

**DEF:** Merit, wholesome actions that bring pleasant results (sukha-vipāka) and lead to favorable rebirths. In AN 8.36, three bases of merit (puñña-kiriya-vatthu) are described: dāna (generosity), sīla (morality), bhāvanā (development/meditation). Puñña creates a foundation for happiness in this and future lives, but does not by itself lead to liberation (nibbāna).

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these three bases of merit. What three? The basis of merit consisting in generosity; the basis of merit consisting in morality; the basis of merit consisting in development." (AN 8.36)

**REL:**
- → Puñña-kiriya-vatthu (bases of merit)
- → Dāna, Sīla, Bhāvanā
- → Kusala (wholesome)
- → Sukha-vipāka (pleasant result)

---

<a name="term-187"></a>
## 187. Saṃsāra

**Skt.: saṃsāra**

**ET:** saṃ- (together) + *√sṛ* (to flow, move) = "wandering," "cycle," "stream of existence."

**DEF:** The cycle of rebirth, the round of existence conditioned by ignorance (avijjā) and craving (taṇhā). In SN 15.1, the Buddha says: "Inconceivable is the beginning of this saṃsāra. Not to be discovered is the first point of beings who, hindered by ignorance and fettered by craving, wander and fare on."

**QUO:**
> "Inconceivable, bhikkhus, is the beginning of this saṃsāra. Not to be discovered is the first point of beings who, hindered by ignorance and fettered by craving, wander and fare on." (SN 15.1)

**REL:**
- → Nibbāna (cessation of saṃsāra)
- → Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent origination)
- → Avijjā (ignorance)
- → Taṇhā (craving)
- → Kāma-loka, Rūpa-loka, Arūpa-loka (three worlds)

---

<a name="term-188"></a>
## 188. Loka

**Skt.: loka**

**ET:** from *√lok* (to see, shine) or *√luj* (to disintegrate) = "world," "universe," "that which is subject to destruction."

**DEF:** World, universe, sphere of existence. In SN 35.82 (Loka Sutta), the Buddha defines loka as that which disintegrates (lujjati), and points out that the "world" is the six internal and external sense bases (saḷāyatana): eye and forms, ear and sounds, etc. Thus, loka is not an external universe but one's own experience arising through the six sense faculties.

**QUO:**
> "And what, bhikkhus, is the world? The eye, bhikkhus, is the world; forms are the world; eye-consciousness is the world; eye-contact is the world..." (SN 35.82)

**REL:**
- → Saḷāyatana (six sense bases)
- → Kāma-loka, Rūpa-loka, Arūpa-loka
- → Tiloka (three worlds)
- → Phassa (contact)

**STG:** Understanding loka as the six sense bases develops at the stage of vipassanā.

---

<a name="term-189"></a>
## 189. Kāma-loka

**Skt.: kāma-loka**

**ET:** kāma (sensual desire) + *loka* (world) = "the world of sensual desires."

**DEF:** The world of sensual desires — the lowest of the three spheres of existence (tiloka). Includes eleven levels: four unhappy planes (apāya) — hell (niraya), animal realm (tiracchānayoni), ghost realm (pettivisaya), demon realm (asura); and seven happy planes — the human world (manussaloka) and six heavens of sense worlds (devaloka). Beings in kāma-loka experience strong sensual desires and attachments.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these three worlds. What three? The world of sensual desires, the world of form, the formless world." (AN 3.69)

**REL:**
- → Rūpa-loka, Arūpa-loka
- → Tiloka (three worlds)
- → Kāma (sensual desire)
- → Deva (deities)
- → Manussaloka (human world)

---

<a name="term-190"></a>
## 190. Rūpa-loka

**Skt.: rūpa-loka**

**ET:** rūpa (form) + *loka* (world) = "the world of form."

**DEF:** The world of form — the second of the three spheres of existence. Consists of sixteen (or seventeen) levels corresponding to the four jhānas. Beings in rūpa-loka have subtle material bodies and experience bliss arising from concentration (jhāna). They are free from gross sensual desires but still have form (rūpa) and attachment to it.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these three worlds. What three? The world of sensual desires, the world of form, the formless world." (AN 3.69)

**REL:**
- → Kāma-loka, Arūpa-loka
- → Jhāna (jhāna)
- → Rūpa (form)
- → Brahmā (Brahma, inhabitant of the form world)
- → Samādhi (concentration)

---

<a name="term-191"></a>
## 191. Arūpa-loka

**Skt.: arūpa-loka**

**ET:** a- (without) + *rūpa* (form) + *loka* (world) = "the formless world."

**DEF:** The formless world — the highest of the three spheres of existence. Consists of four levels corresponding to the four formless attainments (arūpa-samāpatti): the sphere of boundless space (ākāsānañcāyatana), the sphere of boundless consciousness (viññāṇañcāyatana), the sphere of nothingness (ākiñcaññāyatana), the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception (nevasaññānāsaññāyatana). Beings in arūpa-loka have no material form, only consciousness.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these three worlds. What three? The world of sensual desires, the world of form, the formless world." (AN 3.69)

**REL:**
- → Kāma-loka, Rūpa-loka
- → Arūpa-samāpatti (formless attainments)
- → Ākāsānañcāyatana, Viññāṇañcāyatana, Ākiñcaññāyatana, Nevasaññānāsaññāyatana
- → Jhāna (jhāna)

---

<a name="term-192"></a>
## 192. Devatā / Deva

**Skt.: devatā / deva**

**ET:** from *√div* (to shine) = "shining being," "deity."

**DEF:** Deities, shining beings, inhabitants of heavenly worlds. Devas are not omnipotent creators but beings reborn in heavenly realms through wholesome actions (puñña). They possess great abilities, long life, and bliss, but are still subject to death and rebirth. In SN 1 (Devatā-saṃyutta), conversations of the Buddha with various devatā are recorded.

**QUO:**
> "A deity dwelling nearby recited this verse in the presence of the Blessed One..." (standard opening of many suttas in SN 1)

**REL:**
- → Devaloka (heavenly world)
- → Brahmā (Brahma)
- → Sakka (ruler of devas)
- → Puñña (merit leading to deva rebirth)
- → Kāma-loka

---

<a name="term-193"></a>
## 193. Kāyānupassanā

**Skt.: kāyānupassanā**

**ET:** kāya (body) + *anupassanā* (contemplation, observation) = "contemplation of the body."

**DEF:** Contemplation of the body — the first of the four foundations of mindfulness (satipaṭṭhāna). In MN 10 (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta), it includes various practices: mindfulness of breathing (ānāpānasati), awareness of postures (iriyāpatha), full understanding of actions (sampajañña), contemplation of the body's unattractiveness through analysis of parts (paṭikūla-manasikāra), analysis of elements (dhātu-vavatthāna), and contemplation of a corpse at various stages of decomposition (sīvathikā).

**QUO:**
> "And how, bhikkhus, does a bhikkhu dwell contemplating the body as a body? Here, a bhikkhu, when walking, knows: 'I am walking'; when standing, knows: 'I am standing'; when sitting, knows: 'I am sitting'; when lying down, knows: 'I am lying down.'" (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Satipaṭṭhāna (foundations of mindfulness)
- → Ānāpānasati (mindfulness of breathing)
- → Paṭikūla-manasikāra (contemplation of repulsiveness)
- → Dhātu-vavatthāna (analysis of elements)
- → Sampajañña (clear comprehension)
- → Vedanānupassanā, Cittānupassanā, Dhammānupassanā

---

<a name="term-194"></a>
## 194. Vedanānupassanā

**Skt.: vedanānupassanā**

**ET:** vedanā (feeling) + *anupassanā* (contemplation) = "contemplation of feelings."

**DEF:** Contemplation of feelings — the second foundation of mindfulness (satipaṭṭhāna). Observing pleasant (sukha), unpleasant (dukkha), and neutral (adukkhamasukha) feelings at the moment of their arising, presence, and disappearance. The practitioner sees: "A pleasant feeling arises in me" — without identification or reaction.

**QUO:**
> "And how, bhikkhus, does a bhikkhu dwell contemplating feelings as feelings? Here, a bhikkhu, feeling a pleasant feeling, knows: 'I feel a pleasant feeling'; feeling an unpleasant feeling, knows: 'I feel an unpleasant feeling'; feeling a neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant feeling, knows: 'I feel a neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant feeling.'" (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Satipaṭṭhāna (foundations of mindfulness)
- → Vedanā (feeling)
- → Sukha, Dukkha, Adukkhamasukha
- → Taṇhā (craving arising from feelings)
- → Phassa (contact)
- → Vedanākkhandha (aggregate of feeling)

---

<a name="term-195"></a>
## 195. Cittānupassanā

**Skt.: cittānupassanā**

**ET:** citta (mind, state of consciousness) + *anupassanā* (contemplation) = "contemplation of mind."

**DEF:** Contemplation of mind — the third foundation of mindfulness (satipaṭṭhāna). Observing states of mind (citta) at the moment of their arising. In MN 10, sixteen pairs of states are listed: mind with passion (sarāga) or without (vītarāga), with hatred (sadosa) or without (vītadosa), with delusion (samoha) or without (vītamoha), etc.

**QUO:**
> "And how, bhikkhus, does a bhikkhu dwell contemplating mind as mind? Here, a bhikkhu knows a mind with passion as a mind with passion; knows a mind without passion as a mind without passion..." (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Satipaṭṭhāna (foundations of mindfulness)
- → Citta (mind)
- → Kilesa (defilements)
- → Rāga, Dosa, Moha
- → Samādhi (concentration)
- → Vimutti (liberation)

---

<a name="term-196"></a>
## 196. Dhammānupassanā

**Skt.: dharmānupassanā**

**ET:** dhamma (phenomena, teachings) + *anupassanā* (contemplation) = "contemplation of dhammas."

**DEF:** Contemplation of dhammas — the fourth foundation of mindfulness (satipaṭṭhāna). Includes five sections: (1) the five hindrances (nīvaraṇa), (2) the five aggregates (khandha), (3) the six internal and external sense bases (āyatana), (4) the seven factors of awakening (bojjhaṅga), (5) the four noble truths (ariya-sacca).

**QUO:**
> "And how, bhikkhus, does a bhikkhu dwell contemplating dhammas as dhammas in terms of the five hindrances? Here, bhikkhus, when there is sensual desire in him, a bhikkhu knows: 'There is sensual desire in me'..." (MN 10)

**REL:**
- → Satipaṭṭhāna (foundations of mindfulness)
- → Nīvaraṇa (hindrances)
- → Khandha (aggregates)
- → Āyatana (bases)
- → Bojjhaṅga (factors of awakening)
- → Ariya-sacca (noble truths)

<a name="term-197"></a>
## 197. Puñña-kiriya-vatthu

**Skt.: puṇya-kriyā-vastu**

**ET:** puñña (merit) + *kiriya* (action) + *vatthu* (basis, foundation) = "bases for performing meritorious actions."

**DEF:** Three bases for accumulating merit (puñña): dāna (generosity), sīla (morality), bhāvanā (development/meditation). In AN 8.36, they are listed as actions leading to favorable rebirths and happiness in this life.

**QUO:**
> "There are, bhikkhus, these three bases of merit. What three? The basis of merit consisting in generosity; the basis of merit consisting in morality; the basis of merit consisting in development." (AN 8.36)

**REL:**

<a name="term-198"></a>
## 198. Tathāgata

**Skt.: tathāgata**

**ET:** tathā (thus) + *gata* (gone) or *āgata* (come) = "Thus Gone" or "Thus Come." The Buddha left the exact meaning undefined.

**DEF:** An epithet of the Buddha, meaning one who is fully awakened and has attained final liberation. In DN 1 (Brahmajāla Sutta), it is used as a title for the Buddha, contrasted with other teachers who hold wrong views. The Tathāgata is characterized by ten powers (dasa-bala), four confidences (catu-vesārajja), and other unique qualities.

**QUO:**
> "The Tathāgata, bhikkhus, is the arahant, the fully awakened one. Therefore, when the Tathāgata speaks, he knows it to be so and does not distort the truth." (MN 1)

**REL:**
- → Buddha (Buddha)
- → Arahant (arahant)
- → Sugata (Well-gone One)
- → Bhagavā (Blessed One)
- → Sammā-sambuddha (fully awakened one)
- → Dasabala (ten powers of the Tathāgata)

<a name="term-199"></a>
## 199. Ariya

**Skt.: ārya**

**ET:** from *√ar* (to be worthy, to rise) = "noble," "saintly," "exalted."

**DEF:** Noble one — one who has attained at least the first stage of awakening (sotāpatti) and has thus transcended the state of an ordinary person (puthujjana). Four types of noble persons (ariya-puggala) are distinguished: sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī, arahant. Also used in combinations: ariya-sacca (noble truths), ariya-magga (noble path), ariya-saṅgha (noble community).

**QUO:**
> "Because these four truths are noble, they are called noble truths. And because they are realized by nobles, they are called noble truths." (SN 56.21)

**REL:**
- → Sotāpanna, Sakadāgāmī, Anāgāmī, Arahant
- → Ariya-sacca (noble truths)
- → Ariya-magga (noble path)
- → Ariya-saṅgha (noble community)
- → Puthujjana (ordinary person)

---

<a name="term-200"></a>
## 200. Ariya-sacca

**Skt.: ārya-satya**

**ET:** ariya (noble) + *sacca* (truth) = "noble truth."

**DEF:** Four noble truths — the foundation of the Buddha's teaching, proclaimed in the first sermon (SN 56.11, Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta): (1) dukkha (suffering), (2) dukkha-samudaya (origin of suffering — taṇhā, craving), (3) dukkha-nirodha (cessation of suffering — nibbāna), (4) dukkha-nirodha-gāminī paṭipadā (the path leading to the cessation of suffering — the Noble Eightfold Path).

**QUO:**
> "Because these four truths are noble, they are called noble truths. And because they are realized by nobles, they are called noble truths." (SN 56.21)

**REL:**
- → Dukkha (first truth)
- → Taṇhā (craving, second truth)
- → Nibbāna (cessation, third truth)
- → Ariya-aṭṭhaṅgika-magga (Noble Eightfold Path, fourth truth)
- → Dhammacakka (wheel of Dhamma)
- → Pariññā, Pahāna, Sacchikiriya, Bhāvanā (four tasks)

---

<a name="term-201"></a>
## 201. Saṅgha

**Skt.: saṅgha**

**ET:** from *√sañj* (to gather together) = "community," "assembly," "group."

**DEF:** The community of practitioners. Two main types are distinguished: (1) ariya-saṅgha (noble community) — those who have attained at least the first stage of awakening; (2) sammuti-saṅgha (conventional community) — the community of monks (bhikkhu) and nuns (bhikkhunī) who have formally taken monastic vows. As the third Jewel (ti-ratana), the object of refuge is the ariya-saṅgha.

**QUO:**
> "Four pairs, eight persons — this is the Saṅgha of the Blessed One's disciples, worthy of gifts, worthy of hospitality, worthy of offerings, worthy of reverential salutation, the unsurpassed field of merit for the world." (AN 4.87)

**REL:**
- → Ariya-saṅgha (noble community)
- → Sammuti-saṅgha (conventional community)
- → Bhikkhu (monk), Bhikkhunī (nun)
- → Ti-ratana (Triple Gem)
- → Puññakkhetta (field of merit)
- → Sīma (monastic boundary)

## Final Note on Transliteration

This dictionary uses the International Alphabet of Pali Transliteration (IAPT) for all Pāli terms. The pronunciation guide follows simplified English phonetic notation:

- **Vowels:** a = 'u' in "cut", ā = 'a' in "father", i = 'i' in "sit", ī = 'ee' in "see", u = 'u' in "put", ū = 'oo' in "cool", e = 'e' in "get", o = 'o' in "got"
- **Retroflex (ṭ, ḍ, ṇ, ḷ):** as t, d, n, l (tongue curled back)
- **Palatal (c, j, ñ):** ch, j, ny (as in "church", "judge", "canyon")
- **Aspirates (kh, gh, ch, jh, ṭh, ḍh, th, dh, ph, bh):** pronounced with an 'h' release — as in "blockhouse", "doghouse", "church-hill"
